\section{Week 7: Model Adaptation, LoRA, Quantization, and Deployment Reality}

\subsection{Introduction: Why Adaptation Is an Engineering Problem, Not a Research Curiosity}

Weeks~5 and~6 described how to pretrain a base model on tens of trillions of
tokens. That effort is the most expensive single line item in modern AI, and a
typical organization will do it never.
What they will do, dozens of times, is \emph{adapt} a pretrained model to
something more specific: a product domain, a proprietary tone, a regulated
terminology, a safety policy, a pinned behaviour.
This is where most practitioner value is created, and it is also where most
deployment-time failures originate.

This chapter is about the space of techniques that sit between \emph{using
a base model as is} and \emph{retraining it from scratch}.
The modern centre of gravity is parameter-efficient fine-tuning
\citep{houlsby2019adapters, hu2021lora, lialin2023peftsurvey}, combined with
post-training quantization
\citep{dettmers2022llmint8, frantar2023gptq, lin2024awq, xiao2023smoothquant}
and, increasingly, a unification of the two under QLoRA
\citep{dettmers2023qlora}.
Engineers who understand both halves --- the low-rank mathematics of
adapters and the arithmetic of reduced-precision formats --- can move a
foundation model into production at one to two orders of magnitude lower
cost than teams who do not.

The chapter has five aims.
First, to explain precisely why full fine-tuning is the wrong default for
almost every team.
Second, to derive LoRA and its variants from the intrinsic-dimensionality
hypothesis and evaluate when they break.
Third, to develop a disciplined vocabulary for quantization --- FP32, FP16,
BF16, INT8, FP8, INT4/NF4 --- and for the algorithms that compress models
into those formats without destroying them.
Fourth, to describe QLoRA as the production-favourite combination and to
put LLM.int8() and SmoothQuant beside it.
Fifth, to close with the adaptation-and-serving \emph{system}: registries,
adapter composition, regression testing, rollback, and the human-factors
failure modes that dog adaptation projects in regulated environments.

Throughout, we treat a ``model'' not as a single static artefact but as a
pair $(W_0, \Delta)$: a large, rarely-changing base and a small, frequently
changing delta. This reframing --- one base, many deltas --- is the
single most important operational insight in the chapter.

\subsection{Learning Objectives}

By the end of this week, you should be able to:
\begin{itemize}
  \item explain quantitatively why full fine-tuning of a modern LLM is
        infeasible for most teams and operationally undesirable for the rest;
  \item state the intrinsic-dimensionality hypothesis and connect it to the
        low-rank form of LoRA;
  \item derive the parameter count, VRAM footprint, and training FLOPs of a
        LoRA update and contrast them with full fine-tuning;
  \item distinguish LoRA, DoRA, AdaLoRA, adapters, prefix/prompt tuning, and
        BitFit, and pick among them given a concrete deployment constraint;
  \item explain the four main number formats used in LLM serving (FP16, BF16,
        INT8, FP8, INT4/NF4), their ranges, precisions, and failure modes;
  \item describe the algorithmic differences between LLM.int8(),
        SmoothQuant, GPTQ, AWQ, and QLoRA's NF4, and know when each is a
        reasonable default;
  \item design a validation and rollback strategy for an adapted, quantized
        model that behaves non-trivially differently from its base.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Conceptual Core: Why Full Fine-Tuning Breaks Down}

Full fine-tuning updates \emph{all} the parameters of a pretrained model on
a new task or corpus. For a 70B-parameter model at bfloat16, this means
touching roughly 140~GB of parameters, and, through AdamW, maintaining
roughly $6\cdot 70\text{B} = 420\text{G}$ bytes of optimizer state plus
activations.
The total VRAM budget under the Week~6 arithmetic lands between 0.8 and
1.4~TB, which on 80~GB H100s is ten to eighteen GPUs \emph{per training
job, before any data parallelism}.

The compute budget is equally ungenerous. A full epoch over even a
modest one-billion-token finetuning corpus requires roughly $6ND =
6\cdot 70\text{B}\cdot 10^9 = 4.2\cdot 10^{20}$ FLOPs, which is of the
same order as the pretraining budget of GPT-2.
If one believes the Chinchilla recipe (Week~5), one should expect the
compute per parameter per token to be $6$, and the \emph{marginal}
benefit of full fine-tuning at ten or one hundred million tokens to be
very small: the gradient signal per parameter is tiny, and most of the
updates are noise.

Cost is not the only reason full fine-tuning is rare. There are four
more, and they are operational:

\paragraph{Catastrophic forgetting.}
Because every parameter is updated, the model can lose capabilities
that the finetuning corpus does not exercise. A classic pattern is a
model that is instruction-tuned on polite-customer-service data and
then fails at arithmetic or code. Unless your evaluation matrix covers
every capability you care about, full fine-tuning is a way to silently
degrade the product.

\paragraph{Versioning and rollback complexity.}
A fully finetuned model is an entirely new 140~GB artefact. Storing
many of them (one per customer, per domain, per experiment) is
expensive and slow. Rolling back to ``last week's model'' means
reloading 140~GB into GPU memory, not swapping an adapter.

\paragraph{Safety drift.}
Alignment behaviour (Week~8) is encoded diffusely in the base model's
weights. Full fine-tuning overwrites those weights and typically
\emph{undoes} alignment, unless the finetuning data is carefully
mixed with RLHF-trained safety data. This is why unadapter-controlled
fine-tuning is disallowed at most regulated deployments.

\paragraph{Statistical illegibility.}
When every parameter moves, it is hard to answer the question ``what
changed and why.'' The audit trail is a diff of 70B weights. Engineers
and regulators should expect, and demand, adapters that isolate the
change into a small, inspectable delta.

Together, these reasons motivate the modern separation of concerns:
a foundation model is a shared, frozen, rarely-updated asset; a
task-, tenant-, or product-specific adapter is a small, cheap,
inspectable delta.
The whole of this chapter can be read as a formalization of that
separation.

\subsection{Notation and Standing Assumptions}
\label{sec:week7-notation}

\begin{notationbox}
\textbf{Base/adapter pair.}
$W_0 \in \R^{d \times k}$ is a frozen pretrained weight matrix (most
often $d = k = d_{\mathrm{model}}$ for attention projections; $d \ne
k$ for MLP up/down projections). $\Delta W \in \R^{d \times k}$ is
the trainable adaptation delta. For LoRA,
$\Delta W = (\alpha/r)\,B A$ with $B \in \R^{d \times r}$,
$A \in \R^{r \times k}$, rank $r \ll \min(d,k)$, and scale factor
$\alpha > 0$ (typically $\alpha = r$ or $\alpha = 2r$).

\textbf{Adapter budget.}
The per-layer trainable parameter count is $r(d+k)$ for LoRA versus
$dk$ for full fine-tune. For a Llama-style model, summing over all
adapted layers gives $|\Delta|_{\mathrm{LoRA}}$; typical values
$|\Delta|_{\mathrm{LoRA}} \in [10^{6}, 10^{8}]$, to be compared
against $N \in [10^{9}, 10^{11}]$ base parameters.

\textbf{Precision formats.}
Bytes-per-parameter coefficients (extending the Week~2 vocabulary
from Eq.~\eqref{eq:week2-mem-budget}): $b_{\mathrm{fp32}} = 4$,
$b_{\mathrm{bf16}} = b_{\mathrm{fp16}} = 2$, $b_{\mathrm{fp8}} = 1$,
$b_{\mathrm{int8}} = 1$, $b_{\mathrm{int4}} = b_{\mathrm{nf4}} = 0.5$
(one nibble per weight, plus $O(1/32)$ block-scale overhead for NF4).

\textbf{Quantization.}
A quantizer $Q_{\mathrm{fmt}} : \R \to \mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{fmt}}$
maps a full-precision weight to a discrete grid
$\mathcal{G}_{\mathrm{fmt}}$ of $2^{b}$ levels (e.g.\ $b=4$ for INT4
/ NF4, $b=8$ for INT8). Calibration data $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}}$
is a small held-out sample used to fit per-channel scales or
Hessians (GPTQ, AWQ).

\textbf{Standing assumptions.}
\textbf{(A7.1)} Low-rank adaptation is adequate \emph{when} the
fine-tuning task lies within the intrinsic-dimensionality envelope
of $W_0$'s pretraining distribution~\citep{aghajanyan2021intrinsic};
it fails silently outside that envelope (far-domain transfer,
hard-reasoning tasks). Recipes should rank-sweep rather than hardcode
$r$.
\textbf{(A7.2)} Quantization-error propagation through a frozen base
is bounded only up to calibration coverage:
$Q_{\mathrm{fmt}}$'s error on tokens outside
$\mathrm{supp}(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}})$ can be arbitrarily large.
Evaluate on representative production traffic, not only on
$\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}}$.
\textbf{(A7.3)} All ``near-parity'' claims between quantized,
LoRA-adapted, and full-fine-tuned models are \emph{benchmark-
contingent empirical observations} on specific task suites, not
generalization theorems. Far-domain, long-context, and hard-reasoning
regimes may show larger gaps.
\end{notationbox}

\subsection{Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)}

PEFT is the umbrella term for techniques that freeze the pretrained
model $W_0$ and train a small set of \emph{additional} parameters,
usually $\leq 1\%$ of the base \citep{lialin2023peftsurvey}.
The basic empirical finding --- that PEFT can close most of the gap to
full fine-tuning on many tasks --- is a gift from the structure of
overparameterized neural networks, which we will treat in the next
section.

Figure~\ref{fig:peft-landscape} lays out the design space. PEFT
methods divide cleanly into three families.

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \input{figures/peft_landscape.tex}
  \caption{The PEFT landscape. Three families of methods that freeze
  $W_0$: reparameterization (add a low-rank delta), prompt-based (add
  trainable prefix tokens to the input/hidden states), and
  selective/additive (train a small hand-picked subset of parameters
  or insert small modules).}
  \label{fig:peft-landscape}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Reparameterization methods} add a structured low-rank delta
to existing weight matrices. The canonical example is LoRA
\citep{hu2021lora}; later variants include DoRA \citep{liu2024dora}
and AdaLoRA \citep{zhang2023adalora}. These are the \emph{dominant}
family for LLMs today.

\paragraph{Prompt-based methods} do not touch the weights at all.
Instead they learn a \emph{soft prompt} --- a short sequence of
continuous embeddings prepended to the input or to each attention
layer. Prefix tuning \citep{li2021prefix}, prompt tuning
\citep{lester2021prompt}, and P-tuning v2 \citep{liu2022ptuningv2} are
representative. These are cheap and compose well with
retrieval-augmentation (Week~9), but they are sensitive to scale:
prompt tuning beats full fine-tuning only at tens of billions of
parameters \citep{lester2021prompt}.

\paragraph{Selective and additive methods} train a small
hand-selected subset of parameters (BitFit
\citep{zaken2022bitfit}, which trains only biases) or insert small
bottleneck modules (Houlsby adapters \citep{houlsby2019adapters}).
They are operationally simple but add inference latency; LoRA's
appeal over adapters is that the delta can be merged into $W_0$ at
serving time (see \S\ref{subsec:lora-merge}).

The \emph{benefits} of PEFT are uniform across the family:
training fits on a single GPU for most 7B-70B models;
the trainable parameter count drops from tens of billions to tens of
millions; storage per customer drops from hundreds of gigabytes to
hundreds of megabytes; and rollback becomes a file-system operation.
Properly done, PEFT is a safer update, not just a cheaper one.

The rest of the chapter concentrates on LoRA, because it is the
method that most engineers will actually deploy, and because its
mathematics illuminate the rest of the space.

\subsection{Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)}

LoRA \citep{hu2021lora} modifies a linear layer $W_0 x$ in a
pretrained model by adding a trainable low-rank update:

\begin{equation}
  W_0 x \;\longrightarrow\; (W_0 + \Delta W)\,x,
  \qquad
  \Delta W = \frac{\alpha}{r}\, B A,
  \label{eq:lora-core}
\end{equation}

where $W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times k}$ is frozen,
$B \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r\times k}$ are
trainable, and $r \ll \min(d,k)$ is the \emph{rank}. The scalar
$\alpha/r$ is a scale factor that, by convention, decouples the learning
rate from the choice of~$r$.

Figure~\ref{fig:lora-decomp} shows the geometry.

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \input{figures/lora_decomposition.tex}
  \caption{LoRA decomposition. The frozen matrix $W_0$ is a full-rank
  linear map; the update $\Delta W = BA$ lives in an $r$-dimensional
  subspace, requiring only $r(d+k)$ trainable parameters instead of
  $dk$. For a Llama-2-7B query projection ($d=k=4096$, $r=8$), this
  trades 16.8~M parameters for 65~K --- a 256$\times$ reduction.}
  \label{fig:lora-decomp}
\end{figure}

Initialization matters. $A$ is initialised with a normal distribution
(typically scaled like He initialisation) and $B$ is initialised to
\emph{zero}. This ensures $\Delta W = 0$ at step zero, so the
adapted model starts exactly at the pretrained behaviour and
diverges gradually under gradient descent. The asymmetry is deliberate:
if both were random, the initial output would be perturbed and the
model would need to ``un-learn'' the noise before learning the task.

\subsubsection{Parameter and memory budgets}

\begin{derivation}[LoRA parameter compression]
For a single linear layer $W_0 \in \R^{d\times k}$ with
$\Delta W = (\alpha/r) BA$, $B \in \R^{d\times r}$, $A \in \R^{r\times k}$:
\begin{align*}
  |\text{full-FT trainable}| &= dk, \\
  |\text{LoRA trainable}| &= r(d+k), \\
  \rho_{\mathrm{LoRA}} := \frac{|\text{LoRA}|}{|\text{full-FT}|}
  &= \frac{r(d+k)}{dk}
     \;\stackrel{d \approx k}{\approx}\; \frac{2r}{d}
     \;=\; \frac{1}{\min(d,k)/r \cdot (1/2)}.
\end{align*}
For a square matrix, the compression factor is
$1/\rho_{\mathrm{LoRA}} \approx \min(d,k)/(2r)$; for
$d=k=4096$, $r=16$, this is $\approx 128\times$.
\end{derivation}

\Ident\ \emph{LoRA adapter memory budget.} Let $N_{\Delta} = \sum_\ell r_\ell
(d_\ell + k_\ell)$ over adapted layers $\ell$. Using the Week~2 memory
equation (Eq.~\eqref{eq:week2-mem-budget}), for AdamW training in bf16
with fp32 master weights and optimizer state,
\begin{equation}
  M_{\mathrm{LoRA}}
  \;=\;
  \underbrace{b_{\mathrm{bf16}} N}_{\text{frozen base}}
  \;+\;
  \underbrace{(b_{\mathrm{bf16}} + b_{\mathrm{grad}}
            + b_{\mathrm{opt}}) N_{\Delta}}_{\text{adapter + state}}
  \;+\; M_{\mathrm{act}},
  \label{eq:week7-lora-mem}
\end{equation}
where $b_{\mathrm{bf16}}=2$, $b_{\mathrm{grad}}=4$ (fp32 master grad),
$b_{\mathrm{opt}}=8$ (Adam $m,v$ at fp32). For QLoRA, replace the
first term with $b_{\mathrm{nf4}} N = 0.5 N$ plus the $O(N/32)$
block-scale overhead; the second and third terms are unchanged.

\begin{workedexample}[7B and 70B adaptation budgets]
Take Llama-family geometry: $d_{\mathrm{model}}=4096$ (7B) or $8192$ (70B),
$N_{\mathrm{7B}} \approx 7 \cdot 10^9$, $N_{\mathrm{70B}} \approx
7 \cdot 10^{10}$. Apply LoRA at $r=16$ to
$\{W_Q,W_K,W_V,W_O,W_{\mathrm{up}},W_{\mathrm{down}},W_{\mathrm{gate}}\}$
across all 32 (7B) or 80 (70B) layers. Order-of-magnitude arithmetic:

\textbf{7B model, LoRA.} $N_{\Delta} \approx 4\cdot 10^6$.
Frozen base (bf16): $14$~GB. Adapter + state: $14 \cdot 4 \cdot 10^6
= 56$~MB. Activations at batch~1, seq~2048, bf16: $\sim 4$~GB.
\emph{Total $\approx 18$~GB} --- fits on a 24~GB consumer card.

\textbf{7B model, QLoRA.} Base drops to $0.5 \cdot 7 \cdot 10^9 \approx
3.5$~GB plus $\sim 220$~MB scale overhead. \emph{Total
$\approx 8$~GB} --- comfortable on a 12~GB card.

\textbf{70B model, LoRA.} Frozen base (bf16): $140$~GB. Adapter +
state: $\sim 500$~MB. Activations (seq~2048, batch~1): $\sim 12$~GB.
\emph{Total $\approx 153$~GB} --- needs $\geq 2$ H100s.

\textbf{70B model, QLoRA.} Base: $35$~GB + $\sim 2$~GB scales.
\emph{Total $\approx 49$~GB} --- fits on one 80~GB H100.

\textbf{Full FT, 70B.} Memory from Eq.~\eqref{eq:week2-mem-budget}
with $b_p+b_g+b_o=16$: $16 \cdot 7 \cdot 10^{10} = 1.12$~TB plus
activations. \emph{$\geq 16$~H100s per training job} before data
parallelism.

\textbf{Training FLOPs per token.} LoRA gradients only flow through
$\Delta$, but the forward pass touches all $N$ base weights. Per-token
FLOPs stay $\approx 6N$ for forward+backward on LoRA
(dominated by the frozen matmuls), versus $6N$ for full-FT ---
\emph{LoRA's compute savings are small; its memory savings are
large}. This asymmetry is the whole point.
\end{workedexample}

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Why low rank? The intrinsic-dimensionality hypothesis}{Why low rank? The intrinsic-dimensionality hypothesis}}

The empirical licence for a low-rank update comes from
\citet{aghajanyan2021intrinsic}, who showed that pretrained language
models have an \emph{intrinsic dimension} of a few hundred to a few
thousand --- that is, the task-relevant subspace for fine-tuning is
dramatically lower-dimensional than the ambient parameter space.
LoRA's design is a direct operationalization of this finding: if the
useful updates live in an $\approx 10^3$-dimensional subspace, then
training a rank-8 or rank-16 delta should recover most of the
benefit.

\Assum\ (A7.1 in action) The caveat is that ``tasks'' here means tasks
that are close to the pretraining distribution. For \emph{far-domain}
transfer --- a healthcare- or legal-specific model --- the intrinsic
dimension may be higher, and full fine-tuning, or LoRA with much higher
rank, may be required. This is why a production LoRA recipe should
almost always sweep rank $\{4, 8, 16, 32, 64\}$ and pick on validation
loss, rather than hardcoding a number.

\Cons\ \emph{When low-rank fails silently.} The pathological pattern is
this: training loss descends cleanly, validation loss on in-distribution
eval looks competitive, but held-out \emph{generation} on
slightly-off-distribution prompts reverts to base-model behaviour.
Diagnostics: (i)~compute the singular spectrum of the accumulated
LoRA update $\Delta W$ after training; if it is rank-saturated (top-$r$
singular values all of similar magnitude, no knee), the rank is too
low. (ii)~Sweep rank upward; if eval loss continues to improve past
$r=64$, the task is not low-intrinsic-dimensional and LoRA is the
wrong tool. Full fine-tuning or continued pretraining is then
indicated, despite its cost.

\subsubsection{Which layers to adapt?}

\citet{hu2021lora} reported that applying LoRA to \emph{only the
attention projections} $W_Q$ and $W_V$ is competitive with full
fine-tuning on GLUE-scale tasks. This is the ``tight'' LoRA recipe.
Modern practice is more generous: most production recipes apply LoRA
to all four of $\{W_Q, W_K, W_V, W_O\}$ and to the MLP projections
as well, because the MLP layers contain the majority of the
parameter count in a transformer (roughly two thirds for the
Llama-family geometry).

\subsubsection{\label{subsec:lora-merge}Merging at deployment}

A critical operational property of LoRA is that it is a
\emph{linear} update. At serving time one can compute once:
\begin{equation}
  W_\text{merged} = W_0 + \frac{\alpha}{r}\, B A,
\end{equation}
and deploy $W_\text{merged}$ as the forward-pass weight. There is no
inference-time overhead: no extra matmul, no extra KV-cache memory,
no extra latency. This is LoRA's definitive advantage over Houlsby
adapters, which insert bottleneck modules into the forward pass and
therefore cost latency forever.

If the deployment serves many LoRAs against a shared base --- for
example, one per customer tenant --- it is useful \emph{not} to
merge, and instead to keep $W_0$ in HBM once and dispatch the
per-request adapter on demand. This is the pattern implemented by
S-LoRA-style serving stacks, at the cost of a small per-token overhead
for the low-rank matmul.

\subsubsection{Variants}

\paragraph{QLoRA} \citep{dettmers2023qlora} is the most important
variant. The base is quantized to 4-bit NF4 (see \S\ref{subsec:nf4}
below), and the LoRA adapters are trained in bfloat16 on top. The
effect is dramatic: a 65B model that ordinarily requires $\approx
780$~GB of VRAM for full fine-tuning fits on a single 48~GB GPU for
adaptation, with \Emp\ near-parity evaluation scores on the
instruction-following suites reported in the original paper (A7.3:
benchmark-contingent). QLoRA is now the default recipe at many
production shops.

\paragraph{DoRA} \citep{liu2024dora} decomposes the weight update into
magnitude and direction components separately. The claim is that this
better matches the statistics of a full fine-tuning update and
improves low-rank performance, particularly at small $r$.

\paragraph{AdaLoRA} \citep{zhang2023adalora} dynamically allocates
the rank budget across layers, spending more rank where it helps
more. In effect, it treats the total rank as a fixed training-time
budget and reallocates it during training.

These are incremental; the LoRA mainline is not going anywhere.

\subsubsection{Failure modes}

LoRA is not a panacea. Three failure modes recur in practice.

First, rank starvation: if $r$ is too small for the task, the model
underfits, often invisibly --- the training loss looks fine, but
held-out generation drifts back to the base behaviour. The
defence is a rank sweep.

Second, scaling pathology: the $\alpha/r$ scale factor couples
learning rate to rank. Engineers who change $r$ without rethinking
$\alpha$ silently reset the effective learning rate. The common fix
is to set $\alpha = r$ and tune a single learning rate.

Third, layer-selection bias: adapting only attention projections is
cheap but can under-represent the MLP subspaces where facts are
stored \citep{geva2021keyvalue}. Mission-critical domain transfer
usually needs MLP adaptation.

\begin{provedassumed}[LoRA: what is established vs assumed]
\textbf{Proved.} The parameter-count identity
$|\text{LoRA}| = r(d+k)$ is algebraic. The merge identity
$W_0 + (\alpha/r)BA$ is exact; LoRA adds zero inference-time cost
when merged. Initialization $B=0$ yields $\Delta W = 0$ at step~0,
so the adapted model is behaviourally identical to the base at
initialization.\\
\textbf{Empirical.} Near-parity of LoRA with full-FT on
GLUE/instruction tasks at $r \in \{4,\dots,64\}$
\citep{hu2021lora}. Layer-selection benchmarks favouring
attention+MLP over attention-only \citep{hu2021lora,
raschka2023finetuning}. Rank starvation and scaling pathology are
repeated lab findings, not theorems.\\
\textbf{Assumed.} Intrinsic-dimensionality of task-relevant updates
is $\ll \min(d,k)$ (A7.1). This assumption holds for near-domain
adaptation and fails measurably for far-domain and
hard-reasoning targets.
\end{provedassumed}

\subsection{Quantization: The Other Half of Cheap Adaptation}

Quantization reduces the numerical precision with which a model's
weights (and sometimes activations) are stored and computed. It is
the second pillar of affordable LLM deployment. Whereas LoRA reduces
the size of an \emph{update}, quantization reduces the size of the
\emph{base model} it sits on top of.

Figure~\ref{fig:quantization-precision} shows the main number
formats. The axes that matter are \emph{range} (what is the largest
representable finite value?) and \emph{precision} (how finely can
the format distinguish nearby values?).

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \input{figures/quantization_precision.tex}
  \caption{Number formats used in modern LLMs. FP16 sacrifices range
  for precision; BF16 does the opposite, matching FP32's
  $\sim 10^{\pm 38}$ dynamic range but with only 8~bits of mantissa.
  INT8, FP8, and INT4/NF4 trade more precision for dramatically
  smaller footprint.}
  \label{fig:quantization-precision}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{FP32, FP16, BF16, and the rise of mixed precision}

Neural networks were trained in IEEE-754 FP32 for most of the deep
learning era. Two facts about modern LLMs have made FP32 training
obsolete.

First, FP32 stores 23 bits of mantissa. Training gradients for a
transformer almost never need more than $\sim 10$ bits of precision
\citep{micikevicius2018mixed}; the remaining bits are noise.

Second, hardware matrix-multiply units (Tensor Cores on NVIDIA,
Matrix cores on AMD, MXUs on TPUs) are up to $16\times$ faster in
16-bit formats than in 32-bit.

The result was mixed-precision training
\citep{micikevicius2018mixed}: compute forward and backward in FP16
(or BF16), accumulate in FP32, and maintain a FP32 master copy of the
weights for the optimizer. This is the Week~6 arithmetic: weights at
bf16, gradients at bf16, AdamW state at fp32.

FP16 and BF16 differ primarily in range. FP16 (IEEE half-precision)
has 5~bits of exponent and peaks at $\approx 6.5\cdot 10^4$; BF16
has 8~bits of exponent and matches FP32's range. This matters for
LLMs because attention logits $QK^T / \sqrt{d_k}$ routinely hit
magnitudes that overflow FP16 but survive BF16
\citep{kalamkar2019bfloat16}. Most modern training pipelines default
to BF16 for this reason; FP16 survives on older hardware.

\subsubsection{Post-training integer quantization}

Training at FP16/BF16 is one thing; \emph{serving} at INT8 or below
is a different problem. The model was trained assuming continuous
weights; reducing each weight to one of 256 or one of 16 values is a
lossy projection. Naively, the errors compound and output quality
collapses.

The algorithmic trick is to choose the quantization grid carefully,
layer by layer, and in some cases to reshape the activations before
quantization so that the weights fit the grid well.

\paragraph{LLM.int8()} \citep{dettmers2022llmint8} was the first
8-bit scheme that worked reliably for transformers at scale. Its
core observation was that a small number of activation dimensions
(``emergent outliers'') have magnitudes many orders of magnitude
larger than the rest, and that crushing them into INT8 destroys
model quality. LLM.int8() keeps those outlier columns in FP16 and
quantizes only the bulk of the matrix to INT8, with negligible
accuracy loss and $\approx 2\times$ memory savings.

\paragraph{SmoothQuant} \citep{xiao2023smoothquant} addresses the
same outlier problem by \emph{rescaling} activations and weights so
that the outliers migrate from the activation side (hard to
quantize) to the weight side (easier, because they can be absorbed
into per-channel scales). The method enables fully INT8 weights
\emph{and} activations, not just weights, for modest additional
calibration work.

\paragraph{GPTQ} \citep{frantar2023gptq} performs \emph{weight-only}
quantization down to 3--4 bits, using a second-order approximation
(an approximate Hessian built from a small calibration set) to
choose quantization grids that minimize the expected output error.
GPTQ models routinely match the full-precision base on perplexity
metrics while using roughly one quarter of the memory.

\paragraph{AWQ} \citep{lin2024awq} is an activation-aware
alternative: it uses a small calibration set to identify the
$\sim 1\%$ of weight channels that are most sensitive to
quantization error (those most activated by the calibration data)
and leaves them in higher precision. AWQ often ships in deployment
libraries (e.g., TensorRT-LLM, vLLM) because it has especially fast
INT4 kernels.

\paragraph{\label{subsec:nf4}NF4 and double quantization (QLoRA).}
QLoRA \citep{dettmers2023qlora} introduced a 4-bit floating-point
format called NF4 (``normal-float 4-bit'') tuned specifically for
neural network weights, whose distribution is well approximated by
a zero-mean normal.

\begin{derivation}[NF4 as equal-mass quantiles of a unit normal]
\Assum\ After per-block affine normalization, weights within a block
are approximately $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$-distributed. \Def\ Construct the
$2^4 = 16$ representable levels $\{q_0, \dots, q_{15}\}$ so that the
intervals $[q_i, q_{i+1}]$ each carry equal probability mass under
the standard normal. Writing $\Phi$ for the standard-normal CDF, set
\[
  q_i = \Phi^{-1}\!\left(\frac{i + 1/2}{16}\right),
  \qquad i = 0,\dots,15,
\]
with a symmetrized and zero-anchored variant in the actual recipe to
guarantee $0 \in \{q_i\}$ (so that exact-zero weights remain exact
after quantization). \Cons\ Compared to uniform INT4, NF4 spends more
representational resolution near zero, where most weight mass lives,
and less in the tails --- matching the empirical weight histogram of
pretrained LLMs.
\end{derivation}

A second optimization, ``double quantization,'' quantizes the
per-block quantization constants themselves: the block scales are
stored in 8-bit rather than 32-bit, adding another $\approx 0.37$
bits/parameter of saving at negligible error. The combined scheme
delivers 4-bit base weights, 16-bit LoRA adapters, and \Emp\
near-parity downstream evaluation on the evaluation suites reported in
\citet{dettmers2023qlora} (predominantly instruction-following and
zero-shot benchmarks).

\paragraph{FP8 and the emergence of training quantization.}
Recent generations of hardware (H100, Blackwell) natively support
FP8 with two variants: E4M3 (more precision, less range, for
activations) and E5M2 (more range, less precision, for gradients).
FP8 is now used for both training and inference in frontier-scale
labs; it is more forgiving than INT8 because the floating-point
format already concentrates precision near zero, and is increasingly
the default for training-time quantization of optimizer state.

\subsubsection{What can go wrong}

Quantization is \emph{not} safe-by-default. Production teams
have observed all of the following in real systems.

\paragraph{Output drift.}
A quantized model produces different tokens, at different
probabilities, than the unquantized base. Perplexity benchmarks can
be flat while task-specific behaviour regresses. This is why a
quantization rollout should always re-run the adapted-model
evaluation suite, not just the base's.

\paragraph{Rare-token amplification.}
Quantization error is roughly uniform, but the ``cost'' of an error
is not: misquantizing a weight that touches a rare but important
token (a medical term, a code identifier, a proper name) can
disproportionately degrade specialized tasks. Calibration sets
should include representative rare-token data.

\paragraph{Numerical instability in long context.}
Attention scores grow with context length (Week~4). FP8 inference
at 128k context can overflow if the attention softmax accumulator
is not kept at higher precision. Most production stacks keep
softmax in BF16 even when the matmuls are in INT8 or FP8.

\paragraph{Interaction with sampling.}
Quantization flattens the probability distribution slightly. Under
aggressive temperature and top-$p$ sampling (Week~5), the quantized
model can produce outputs that the unquantized model would never
have produced, because low-probability tokens get boosted above the
nucleus threshold. This is an argument for fixing sampling
parameters \emph{after} quantization, not before.

\subsection{QLoRA: The Production Default}

QLoRA \citep{dettmers2023qlora} combines the two halves. The recipe
is:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Quantize the base model to 4-bit NF4, with double quantization
        of the per-block scales.
  \item Freeze the quantized base.
  \item Insert LoRA adapters at attention and MLP projections, in
        BF16.
  \item Train only the adapters; the base is read-only during
        forward and backward passes (and dequantized on the fly
        into BF16 for matmul).
  \item At serving time, either keep the adapter separate (for
        multi-tenant serving), or dequantize and merge (for
        single-model serving).
\end{enumerate}

The result is that a single consumer-class GPU can fine-tune a 65B
model, and a single 80~GB H100 can fine-tune much larger ones. \Emp\
The extensive evaluation in \citet{dettmers2023qlora} shows that
QLoRA's downstream quality is statistically indistinguishable from
16-bit LoRA's, which in turn is close to full fine-tuning's, which
in turn is close to pretrained-base's on most zero-shot tasks
\emph{on the evaluation suites used in that paper} --- the A7.3
caveat applies, and far-domain or hard-reasoning tasks can show
measurable gaps. The combined stack is a dramatic democratization of
LLM adaptation for tasks within the near-domain, near-capability
envelope.

From a systems perspective, the important QLoRA property is
\emph{separability}. The 4-bit base lives once in HBM; hundreds of
adapters (one per tenant, experiment, or policy) live on disk as
small files. Swapping adapters is a cheap operation. This enables
multi-tenant serving architectures that were previously infeasible.

\begin{provedassumed}[Quantization and QLoRA: what is established vs assumed]
\textbf{Proved.} The bit-width arithmetic: NF4 base + fp16 scales +
double-quantized 8-bit meta-scales gives $\approx 4.37$ bits per base
weight. Per-block affine dequantization $w \approx s \cdot q$ is exact
for the representable grid.\\
\textbf{Empirical.} QLoRA downstream quality $\approx$ 16-bit LoRA
$\approx$ 16-bit full-FT on instruction-following benchmarks
\citep{dettmers2023qlora}; LLM.int8() preserves perplexity
at $\approx 2\times$ memory saving
\citep{dettmers2022llmint8}; GPTQ 4-bit preserves perplexity on
common benchmarks \citep{frantar2023gptq}; AWQ competitive with
GPTQ at faster INT4 kernels \citep{lin2024awq}. All of these are
\emph{benchmark-contingent} claims (A7.3) --- representative of a
task distribution, not theorems on all tasks.\\
\textbf{Assumed.} (A7.2) Quantization error on tokens outside the
support of $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}}$ is bounded. (A7.1) Low-rank
adequacy of the adapter. Near-parity claims do \emph{not} generalize
uniformly to hard-reasoning, long-context, or far-domain workloads;
production evaluation must cover the actual deployed task
distribution.
\end{provedassumed}

\subsection{Adaptation Data and the Instruction-Tuning Overlap}

A recurring engineering question is: what data do I train my adapter
on? This chapter deliberately leaves full RLHF, DPO, and
constitutional-AI treatments to Week~8, but the data-engineering
discipline carries over.

Three common patterns, with their uses and failure modes.

\paragraph{Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on curated (prompt, response)
pairs.} The bread-and-butter of PEFT. A few thousand high-quality
examples often suffice; quality matters more than quantity. Failure
modes include style imitation without understanding (the model
learns to sound like a domain expert without becoming one) and
pattern-collapse (the model repeats the format of the training
examples even when inappropriate). The fix is to diversify both
prompts and response styles and to include negative or
counter-example outputs where possible.

\paragraph{Continued pretraining on in-domain text.} When the target
domain is linguistically distant from the base's pretraining corpus
(legal, biomedical, code, industrial telemetry), a round of
next-token training on raw in-domain text before SFT can close the
gap. This is often done under QLoRA, with rank 64--128 and a small
learning rate. The danger is catastrophic forgetting: continued
pretraining on a narrow corpus can cause the model to lose its
general-English fluency. Mixing $\sim 10\%$ of general-domain text
into the continued-pretraining corpus mitigates this.

\paragraph{Preference fine-tuning on (prompt, chosen, rejected)
triples.} The subject of Week~8. DPO \citep{rafailov2023direct} and
related algorithms can be run as LoRA adapters on top of a
supervised fine-tune, giving a three-stage pipeline: base $\to$
LoRA-SFT $\to$ LoRA-DPO that is entirely composable.

All three patterns share the same data-engineering rule: garbage in,
garbage entrenched. Adapters are small enough that they cannot
redistribute a large model's knowledge; they can only tune its
output distribution. Bad data fine-tunes a model into bad habits
with disturbing efficiency.

\subsection{The Adaptation-and-Serving System}

Beyond the per-adapter mathematics lies a system design problem.
Most organizations that adapt LLMs do not do so once; they do so
dozens of times per week, across teams, projects, and customers. The
systems engineering challenge is to make this sustainable and safe.

\paragraph{Registries and lineage.} Every adapter should be traceable
to (i) the base model version it was trained against, (ii) the
training data snapshot, (iii) the hyperparameters and random seed,
(iv) the evaluation results, and (v) the human approver. A common
implementation is a model registry (MLflow, Weights \& Biases, or a
home-grown S3-plus-metadata scheme) with strict schemas. Without
this, organizations lose track of which adapters are safe and end up
deploying untested combinations.

\paragraph{Composition.} Adapters compose linearly: if $\Delta_1$
and $\Delta_2$ are both LoRA deltas on the same base, then
$W_0 + \Delta_1 + \Delta_2$ is a valid merged model. In practice,
this works when the adapters target complementary behaviours (e.g.,
domain + style) and fails when they conflict (e.g., two competing
style adapters). A careful engineering discipline here is to train
each adapter against the \emph{same} base and to benchmark the
composed model separately.

\paragraph{Multi-tenant serving.} Inference stacks like vLLM
\citep{kwon2023vllm} now support LoRA dispatch: the base lives once
on each GPU, and per-request adapters are paged in as needed. This
can serve hundreds of adapters on a single node with modest overhead
($\sim$5--10\% per-token latency), and it is the architecture of
choice for SaaS deployments with many tenants.

\paragraph{Rollback.} One of the defining safety properties of PEFT
is that rollback is cheap. Swapping an adapter file takes
milliseconds; restoring an older fully fine-tuned model takes tens
of seconds to a minute. This makes PEFT deployments fundamentally
more recoverable from bad updates, and should be exploited
explicitly in the deployment plan: all adapters should be versioned,
and all rollouts should be reversible with a single configuration
change.

\paragraph{Shadow and A/B testing.} An adapted model should be run
in shadow mode against the previous model for a statistically
meaningful window before full traffic is shifted. Evaluation should
include task-specific metrics, safety tripwires, user-facing
feedback signals, and (crucially) \emph{distribution-shift
detection} on the input side: a new adapter that is wonderful on
yesterday's traffic may be disastrous on today's.

\subsection{Systems Engineering Implications}

\textbf{Requirements.}
The systems requirement is composability and reversibility. A
production LLM platform that is hard to adapt will fail to keep up
with business needs; one that is hard to roll back will accumulate
safety debt.

\textbf{Architecture.}
The base model is a frozen, rarely-updated shared asset; adapters
are small, inspectable, per-tenant deltas. Multi-tenant serving
dispatches adapters on demand. Quantization of the base unlocks a
substantially larger effective GPU fleet by reducing HBM pressure.

\textbf{Verification and validation.}
Every adapter is tested against (i) the base-model regression suite,
(ii) the task-specific eval suite, (iii) a safety tripwire suite
inherited from Week~8, and (iv) a representative sample of
production prompts run in shadow mode. Quantized variants re-run all
four suites, not just a perplexity check.

\textbf{Operations.}
Adapters are versioned and signed. Rollouts are gradual and
reversible. The runtime monitors for output-drift alerts
(distribution changes in token frequency, refusal rates, length) and
for cost anomalies (quantization regressions that change FLOPs
indirectly through longer generations).

\subsection{Human Factors and Failure Modes}

Adaptation attracts distinctive misunderstandings that the engineer
should be prepared to correct.

\paragraph{``The model has been retrained.''}
Adapter-based fine-tuning does \emph{not} retrain the model; it
trains a small additional delta. The base is unchanged and its
capabilities, liabilities, and alignment are unchanged. This is
usually framed positively (``my adapter inherits the safety
training'') but sometimes negatively (``my adapter inherits the
base's factual errors'').

\paragraph{``Quantization only affects speed.''}
Quantization changes outputs. It must be evaluated, not just
benchmarked for throughput. Quality teams that treat quantization
as a serving-side optimization without end-to-end testing tend to
discover regressions in production.

\paragraph{``LoRA is free.''}
Merged LoRAs are free at inference, but unmerged LoRAs and
multi-tenant adapter dispatch cost bandwidth and, often, small
latency. Product teams that underestimate this cost oversell
per-tenant personalization as a zero-cost feature.

\paragraph{``More rank is better.''}
Higher-rank LoRAs have more capacity but also more tendency to
overfit small adaptation corpora and to dilute alignment. The right
rank is empirical.

\paragraph{``QLoRA equals LoRA equals FT.''}
Evaluation studies show that the gap is usually small, but it is
not always negligible --- particularly for hard reasoning tasks and
for far-domain transfer. An engineer pushing the edge of what an
adapted model can do should always benchmark against a full-FT
baseline, even if the production recipe is QLoRA.

\subsection{Key References}

The LoRA and QLoRA papers \citep{hu2021lora, dettmers2023qlora} are
mandatory primary reading for any engineer deploying adapter-based
fine-tuning. The intrinsic-dimensionality paper
\citep{aghajanyan2021intrinsic} gives the theoretical motivation.
The PEFT survey \citep{lialin2023peftsurvey} is the broadest
available overview of the design space.
For quantization, the four algorithmic pillars are LLM.int8()
\citep{dettmers2022llmint8}, SmoothQuant \citep{xiao2023smoothquant},
GPTQ \citep{frantar2023gptq}, and AWQ \citep{lin2024awq}; read them
in that order. The mixed-precision \citep{micikevicius2018mixed}
and BF16 \citep{kalamkar2019bfloat16} papers are the background
for all LLM training precision decisions. Houlsby adapters
\citep{houlsby2019adapters}, prefix tuning \citep{li2021prefix},
prompt tuning \citep{lester2021prompt}, and BitFit
\citep{zaken2022bitfit} are the mainline PEFT alternatives. For a
production serving stack, \citet{kwon2023vllm} describes the
PagedAttention architecture that underpins modern multi-tenant
LoRA dispatch. The Hugging Face PEFT library
\citep{mangrulkar2023peft} is the dominant open-source
implementation.

\subsection{Practitioner Lab}

A concrete lab that mirrors this chapter's progression, runnable on
a single 24~GB consumer GPU:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Load a 7B base model in BF16 and benchmark (a) VRAM
        footprint, (b) throughput in tokens/s at batch~1, (c)
        end-to-end latency for a 256-token generation.
  \item Re-load the same base in 4-bit NF4 (QLoRA-style loading)
        and repeat the benchmarks. Record the tradeoff.
  \item On a small SFT dataset (e.g., Dolly or a curated subset of
        Alpaca), train a LoRA adapter at $r\in\{4, 16, 64\}$
        targeting $\{W_Q, W_V\}$. Log training loss and held-out
        eval loss.
  \item Repeat step~3 targeting $\{W_Q, W_K, W_V, W_O, \text{MLP}\}$.
        Compare to the attention-only variant at equal rank.
  \item Apply QLoRA ($r=16$ adapter on a 4-bit base) on the same
        data; compare to the BF16 LoRA in step~3.
  \item Merge the $r=16$ attention+MLP adapter into the base and
        re-benchmark latency. Confirm it matches the unadapted
        throughput.
  \item Evaluate all four adapters on a held-out eval suite (one
        generation task, one classification task, one safety
        tripwire). Tabulate the accuracy/safety/latency tradeoffs.
  \item Choose one configuration and run a shadow A/B against the
        unadapted base on 1,000 representative prompts. Measure
        output-drift metrics (token frequency, length distribution,
        refusal rate) and decide on a rollout or rollback.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Week 7 Takeaway}

\begin{quote}
Most real-world LLM value comes from safely \emph{adapting} a
pretrained base, not retraining it. LoRA in its QLoRA form, combined
with disciplined quantization and a registry-backed serving stack, is
today's production default. The engineer's job is to enforce the
separation of concerns --- shared base, small adapters, inspectable
diffs --- and to evaluate each adapter as rigorously as the base it
modifies.
\end{quote}
