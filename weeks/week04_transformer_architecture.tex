\section{Week 4: The Transformer --- Attention as the Core Computational Primitive}
\label{sec:week4-transformer}

\subsection*{Setting the Scene}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Setting the Scene}

\paragraph{The problem.}
A sentence is a sequence in which almost any word can, in principle,
depend on almost any other. The pronoun in the last clause may refer
to a noun in the first. The verb tense at the end of a paragraph may
be set by an adverb at the beginning. If you want to predict the next
word in a passage, you need an architecture in which information from
arbitrary distances in the past can reach the present without being
attenuated, garbled, or forgotten. The architecture must also be
trainable on hardware that prefers to do many things at once. Those
two requirements --- arbitrary-range memory and parallel
computation --- are in tension, and the history of sequence
modelling is largely the history of trying to satisfy both at once.

\paragraph{How we got here.}
The earliest neural sequence models were recurrent. Jeffrey Elman's
1990 paper, with the unpretentious title \emph{Finding Structure in
Time}, defined a network that maintained a hidden state and updated
it one token at a time. The hidden state was meant to summarize the
past. In practice, it did so badly: gradients flowing back through
many time steps either vanished, leaving the network unable to
learn long-range dependencies, or exploded, leaving it unable to
learn anything at all. Sepp Hochreiter and J\"urgen Schmidhuber
diagnosed this in 1997 and proposed the Long Short-Term Memory cell,
which used carefully gated arithmetic to let some signals pass
through unmolested. The LSTM was, for two decades, the workhorse of
sequence modelling.

The next move came in machine translation. Ilya Sutskever, Oriol
Vinyals, and Quoc Le showed in 2014 that a pair of LSTMs could
translate sentences end to end: one LSTM encoded the source
sentence into a fixed-length vector, and another LSTM decoded that
vector into the target sentence. The trick almost worked. The
catch was the fixed-length vector, which had to compress the entire
source sentence into a few thousand numbers regardless of how long
the sentence was. For short sentences this was fine; for longer
ones it was disastrous. Dzmitry Bahdanau, Kyunghyun Cho, and Yoshua
Bengio responded in 2015 with what they called \emph{attention}: at
each step of the decoder, instead of relying on the bottleneck
vector, the model would compute a weighted sum over all the
encoder's hidden states, with the weights chosen by the decoder
itself. The bottleneck disappeared. Translations got better.

The Transformer, introduced in 2017 by Ashish Vaswani and
collaborators at Google in a paper titled \emph{Attention Is All You
Need}, took the next step. They asked, in effect: if attention is
the part that works, what happens if we throw away the recurrence
entirely? The answer was that you got a model that could be trained
in parallel across an entire sequence, whose gradients did not
attenuate exponentially with sequence distance the way recurrent
state transitions had, and that scaled, when given more data
and more parameters, in ways the recurrent models had never
managed. Every Large Language Model in production today is a
descendant of that paper.

\paragraph{Where we stand.}
The modern Transformer is a stack of identical blocks, each of which
does two things and then adds the result to a running sum (the
\emph{residual stream}). The first thing is self-attention: every
token computes a query, every other token computes a key and a
value, the query and keys are dotted together to produce a similarity
score, the scores are normalized into weights, and each token's new
representation is the weighted sum of the values. The whole
operation is a content-addressable lookup, scaled by
$1/\sqrt{d_k}$ to keep the dot products from saturating the softmax,
and masked so that each token can attend only to earlier positions
and to itself --- never to future tokens --- during training. The second thing each block does is run the result through
a position-wise feed-forward network --- a small two-layer MLP
applied independently to each token. Around both operations,
modern designs put a normalization layer (LayerNorm or its cheaper
cousin RMSNorm) before the operation rather than after, a choice
called \emph{pre-norm} that turns out to make very deep stacks
trainable. Position information, which the attention mechanism does
not encode on its own, is added separately --- originally as fixed
sinusoids, more recently through rotary position embeddings (RoPE)
that have a clean relative-position interpretation. To save memory
during inference, modern designs share keys and values across groups
of heads (Grouped-Query Attention) and store the keys and values of
past tokens in a cache that grows linearly with context length.
That, with thousands of careful engineering decisions on top, is the
present state of the art.

\paragraph{What goes wrong.}
The attention mechanism is quadratic in the sequence length. The
score matrix has $T^2$ entries, and at long contexts that becomes
the dominant cost both in compute and in memory. A great deal of
recent engineering --- FlashAttention, sliding windows, sparse
attention, state-space hybrids --- exists to push back against this
cost, with mixed success. Position encodings that worked beautifully
inside the training range often degrade outside it; a model trained
on 4{,}000-token contexts will not, in general, behave well on
40{,}000-token contexts without some form of position-extrapolation
surgery. The model's attention can collapse onto a few high-norm
``sink'' tokens that the architecture seems to use as registers,
distorting any interpretation that treats attention weights as
explanations of behavior. And there is the well-documented
\emph{lost-in-the-middle} effect: a model handed a long document
will reliably attend to the beginning and end and underweight the
middle, regardless of where the answer actually lies. The
mathematics of this chapter --- the variance-preserving scale, the
softmax denominator, the RoPE rotation, the KV-cache budget ---
will give you the tools to predict where each of these failure modes
comes from and which architectural choice each one is the price of.

\subsection{Notation and Standing Assumptions}
\label{sec:week4-notation}

\begin{notationbox}
\textbf{Shapes.}
$T$: context length (tokens). $d$: model / residual-stream width
(also $d_{\mathrm{model}}$). $H$: number of attention heads.
$d_k$: per-head query/key dimension, typically $d_k = d/H$.
$d_v$: per-head value dimension, typically $d_v = d_k$.
$G$: number of key/value groups in GQA, with $G \mid H$; $G = H$
recovers MHA, $G = 1$ recovers MQA.
$L$: number of Transformer blocks (layers).
$d_f$: FFN hidden width, $4d$ classic / $\tfrac{8}{3}d$ for SwiGLU.
$b$: bytes per tensor element ($b{=}2$ for bf16/fp16).

\textbf{Matrices.}
$X \in \R^{T\times d}$ token activations. $Q,K \in \R^{T\times d_k}$
per-head query/key; $V \in \R^{T\times d_v}$. $A \in \R^{T\times T}$
post-softmax attention weights. $W_Q, W_K \in \R^{d\times d_k}$,
$W_V \in \R^{d\times d_v}$, $W_O \in \R^{H d_v \times d}$.

\textbf{Standing assumptions.}
\textbf{(A4.1)} The variance-preserving scaling argument for
$1/\sqrt{d_k}$ assumes $Q_{ij}, K_{ij}$ have zero mean, unit
variance, and are pairwise independent across the summation index
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:week4-qk-var-assumption} below). These conditions
hold only at initialization; during training, the variance is
empirically regularized by gradient flow but no longer by
independence.
\textbf{(A4.2)} RoPE's relative-position identity
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:week4-rope-relative}) is \emph{exact} under exact
arithmetic, but extrapolation beyond the training range relies on
the further \emph{empirical} assumption that learned query/key
statistics generalize to unseen rotation angles --- which they do
only partially without interpolation surgery (NTK-aware, YaRN).
\textbf{(A4.3)} Head-specialization, induction-head formation, and
attention-pattern interpretations are \Emp\ empirical phenomena
\citep{elhage2021mathematical, olsson2022induction}, not
architectural guarantees. They are training-outcome dependent and
can regress under recipe changes.
\end{notationbox}

\subsection{Conceptual Core: Why Attention Replaced Recurrence}

Prior sequence models (RNNs, LSTMs, GRUs) compute each hidden state
$h_t$ from $h_{t-1}$. This imposes three structural limits:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Serial compute}: the $t$-th step cannot begin until the
        $(t-1)$-th step finishes; the per-sequence wall-clock is
        $\Theta(T)$ even on infinite parallelism.
  \item \textbf{Long-range signal decay}: gradients propagate through
        $T$ multiplicative updates, producing vanishing or exploding
        long-range signals.
  \item \textbf{Limited effective memory}: the hidden state is a
        compressed summary of the past and has fixed capacity.
\end{itemize}
The Transformer \citep{vaswani2017attention} eliminates recurrence
entirely. Each token attends directly to every other token in the
context window via a content-addressable lookup. The resulting
architecture is fully parallel across the sequence axis, has
constant-depth signal paths, and is in principle \emph{addressable}
across the full context length $T$. Whether information at position
$j$ is reliably \emph{recalled} when needed at position $i$ is an
empirical question --- the lost-in-the-middle phenomenon
(\citealt{liu2024lost}, Week~9) shows that addressability and
reliable recall come apart in practice.

\subsection{Scaled Dot-Product Self-Attention}

\subsubsection{Setup}

Let the Transformer see a contextualized input
$X \in \mathbb{R}^{T\times d}$ (one row per token, $T$ tokens in the
context, model dimension $d$). Each attention layer projects $X$ into
\emph{queries}, \emph{keys}, and \emph{values}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week4-qkv}
Q = X W_Q, \qquad
K = X W_K, \qquad
V = X W_V,
\end{equation}
with $W_Q, W_K \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d_k}$ and
$W_V \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d_v}$. So $Q, K \in \mathbb{R}^{T\times d_k}$
and $V \in \mathbb{R}^{T\times d_v}$. The output is a weighted
combination of the value rows:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week4-attn}
\mathrm{Attn}(Q, K, V)
  \;=\; \underbrace{\mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q K^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}} \;+\; M\right)}_{A \in \mathbb{R}^{T\times T}}\,
        V,
\end{equation}
where $M$ is the causal mask (\S\,Causal Masking) with $M_{ij} = 0$
for $j \le i$ and $M_{ij} = -\infty$ for $j > i$. Figure
\ref{fig:week4-attn-scores} diagrams the computation.

  Eq.~\eqref{eq:week4-attn}. Queries $Q$ and keys $K^{\top}$ multiply

  \times T}$, which is scaled by $\sqrt{d_k}$
  (Eq.~\eqref{eq:week4-qk-sum}), masked, softmax-normalized, and

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Why $\sqrt{d_k}$?}{Why sqrt(d\_k)?}}

\begin{derivation}[Variance-preserving score scaling]
\Assum\ (A4.1): $Q_{im}, K_{jm}$ are random variables with
$\E[Q_{im}] = \E[K_{jm}] = 0$, $\Var[Q_{im}] = \Var[K_{jm}] = 1$,
and $Q_{im} \perp K_{jm'}$ for all $m,m'$. We additionally assume
that, within each of $Q$ and $K$, the head-dimension entries are
uncorrelated across $m$ (i.e.\ $\mathrm{Cov}(Q_{im},Q_{im'}) = 0$ for
$m\neq m'$ and similarly for $K$), so that the products
$Q_{im}K_{jm}$ are uncorrelated across $m$ and $\Var$ is additive
in the sum below.
\label{eq:week4-qk-var-assumption}

Each score entry is a sum over the head dimension:
\begin{equation}
(QK^{\top})_{ij} \;=\; \sum_{m=1}^{d_k} Q_{im} K_{jm}.
\label{eq:week4-qk-sum}
\end{equation}
By independence and zero mean, each summand satisfies
$\E[Q_{im}K_{jm}] = \E[Q_{im}]\E[K_{jm}] = 0$ and
$\Var[Q_{im}K_{jm}] = \E[Q_{im}^2]\E[K_{jm}^2] - 0 = 1$. Summing $d_k$
such uncorrelated zero-mean unit-variance random variables, and
using $\Var[\sum X_m] = \sum \Var[X_m]$ under uncorrelatedness, gives
\begin{equation}
\Var\!\left[(QK^{\top})_{ij}\right] \;=\; \sum_{m=1}^{d_k} 1 \;=\; d_k,
\qquad
\mathrm{sd}\!\left[(QK^{\top})_{ij}\right] = \sqrt{d_k}.
\end{equation}
Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ restores unit variance:
$\Var[(QK^{\top}/\sqrt{d_k})_{ij}] = 1$.
\end{derivation}

\Cons\ Without rescaling, logit magnitudes scale like $\sqrt{d_k}$;
post-softmax this drives attention toward one-hot distributions
and the gradient through the softmax vanishes, because
$\partial \mathrm{softmax}_i / \partial z_j = s_i(\delta_{ij} - s_j)$
is largest near the uniform distribution and collapses toward zero
as $s_i$ approaches~0 or~1. \Heur\ A4.1's independence assumption
breaks mid-training --- learned $Q/K$ distributions are correlated ---
but the dimensional-analysis argument survives: the $\sqrt{d_k}$ scale
is empirically robust across head sizes because it is the only
dimensionally correct way to decouple logit magnitude from $d_k$.

\subsubsection{Interpretation: Differentiable Lookup}

Row $i$ of $A$ is a probability distribution over context positions;
row $i$ of the output is the convex combination of value rows weighted
by that distribution. Pedagogically:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{queries} ask ``what information do I need?'',
  \item \emph{keys} advertise ``I carry this kind of information'',
  \item \emph{values} carry the information itself.
\end{itemize}
Attention is therefore a differentiable, content-addressable read
against a key-value store of size $T$.

\subsubsection{Compute and Memory}

For a single attention head the forward pass costs (counting one
multiply--add as $2$ FLOPs, the convention used throughout this
chapter):
\begin{itemize}
  \item $Q, K, V$ projections: $3 \cdot 2 T d\, d_k = 6 T d\, d_k$ FLOPs.
  \item $Q K^{\top}$: $2 T^2 d_k$ FLOPs.
  \item Softmax: $O(T^2)$ FLOPs (independent of the MAC convention).
  \item $A V$: $2 T^2 d_v$ FLOPs.
\end{itemize}
Total: $O(T d\, d_k + T^2 d_k)$. The quadratic $T^2$ term dominates
for long contexts; the $T d d_k$ term dominates for short contexts.
Memory is $O(T^2)$ for the attention matrix $A$ -- the target of
FlashAttention (\S\,FlashAttention below).

\begin{workedexample}[Full-block FLOP budget (Llama-2-7B geometry)]
Fix $T=2048$, $d=4096$, $H=32$, $d_k=d_v=128$, $d_f = 11008$
(SwiGLU), and count FLOPs per block forward pass (count a multiply-
add as 2 FLOPs; the dominant terms only):

\textbf{Attention.} All four projections $W_Q,W_K,W_V,W_O$ are
$d \times d$: $4 \cdot 2 T d^2 = 8 T d^2 \approx 8 \cdot 2048 \cdot
4096^2 \approx 2.75 \cdot 10^{11}$~FLOPs.
Score matrix $Q K^{\top}$ (all heads together): $2 T^2 d
\approx 2 \cdot 2048^2 \cdot 4096 \approx 3.44 \cdot 10^{10}$.
$A V$: another $2 T^2 d \approx 3.44 \cdot 10^{10}$.
\emph{Attention subtotal:} $\approx 3.44 \cdot 10^{11}$~FLOPs.

\textbf{FFN (SwiGLU).} Three matrices $W_g, W_u \in \R^{d_f\times d}$
(up) and $W_2 \in \R^{d\times d_f}$ (down):
$3 \cdot 2 T d\, d_f \approx 6 \cdot 2048 \cdot 4096 \cdot 11008
\approx 5.54 \cdot 10^{11}$~FLOPs.

\textbf{Block total:} $\approx 3.44 \cdot 10^{11} + 5.54 \cdot
10^{11} = 9.0 \cdot 10^{11}$~FLOPs. With 32 blocks, per-sequence
forward is $\approx 2.9 \cdot 10^{13}$~FLOPs. Checking against the
$6ND$ training-FLOP rule ($N = 7 \cdot 10^9$, one forward + two
backward passes at $D = T = 2048$ tokens): $6ND \approx 8.6 \cdot
10^{13}$, consistent with our forward count being roughly one third
of training FLOPs. \Ident\ FFN dominates the FLOP budget
($\approx 62\%$ of block cost at $T=2048$); attention dominates at
longer $T$ because of the $T^2$ scaling.
\end{workedexample}

\subsection{Causal Masking}

For autoregressive language modeling, token $i$ must not attend to
tokens at position $j > i$, otherwise training leaks the target.
The canonical mask is
\begin{equation}
M_{ij} \;=\;
\begin{cases}
0            & j \le i, \\
-\infty      & j > i,
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
added to the pre-softmax logits. Adding $-\infty$ before softmax zeros
the corresponding weight. Two practical gotchas:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Padding masks} must also be applied so that padded
        positions cannot be attended to \emph{or} attend out, or the
        padding token's hidden state will corrupt neighbors during
        training. The combined mask is the elementwise max of the
        causal and padding masks (after mapping ``masked'' to
        $-\infty$).
  \item \textbf{Block-diagonal masks} are used when multiple
        independent sequences are packed into a single batch (common
        in pretraining for throughput); without the block-diagonal
        pattern tokens leak across document boundaries.
\end{itemize}
Incorrect masking is an insidious failure mode: training loss looks
fine for a while but generalization collapses. Mask correctness is
worth a unit test.

\subsection{Multi-Head Attention}

A single attention head can only attend through one learned
$(Q, K, V)$ decomposition at a time. Multi-head attention
\citep{vaswani2017attention} runs $H$ heads in parallel with different
projections and concatenates the results:
\begin{align}
h_i &= \mathrm{Attn}(X W_Q^{(i)}, X W_K^{(i)}, X W_V^{(i)}),
      \qquad i = 1, \ldots, H, \\
\mathrm{MHA}(X) &= \bigl[h_1 \,|\, h_2 \,|\, \cdots \,|\, h_H\bigr]\, W_O,
\label{eq:week4-mha}
\end{align}
with $W_O \in \mathbb{R}^{H d_v \times d}$. Typically $d_k = d_v = d/H$
so the parameter count matches a single full-size head. Figure
\ref{fig:week4-mha} illustrates.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering

\caption{Multi-head attention, governing Eq.~\eqref{eq:week4-mha}.
  $H$ independent attention heads (each following
  Eq.~\eqref{eq:week4-attn}) operate in parallel over the same input
  $X$; their outputs are concatenated along the feature axis and
  projected back to model dimension $d$ by $W_O$.}
\label{fig:week4-mha}

\Emp\ Heads specialize \citep{elhage2021mathematical}: some attend to
adjacent positions (n-gram heads), some to syntactic dependencies,
some to previously-seen tokens of the same type (induction heads,
\citealt{olsson2022induction}, revisited in Week~5). This
specialization is not engineered in; it \emph{emerges} from training
(A4.3) and can regress under recipe changes --- it is an observation,
not a guarantee.

\subsubsection{Multi-Query and Grouped-Query Attention}

At inference time, multi-head attention keeps a separate key/value
cache per head (see \S\,KV Cache), which dominates memory. Two
architectural mitigations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Multi-Query Attention (MQA)} \citep{shazeer2019fast}:
        share a single $K, V$ pair across all $H$ heads; each head
        keeps its own $Q$. Reduces KV cache by $H\times$ with modest
        quality loss.
  \item \textbf{Grouped-Query Attention (GQA)}
        \citep{ainslie2023gqa}: partition the $H$ query heads into
        $G$ groups, each sharing a single $(K, V)$; recovers most of
        the quality of MHA while keeping KV cache size at $G$-per-token.
        $G \in \{4, 8\}$ is common in modern LLMs (Llama~2/3, Mistral).
\end{itemize}
For inference-optimized workloads with long contexts and large
batch sizes --- the typical serving regime --- GQA is generally
the better engineering default: the quality cost is small in
benchmarks reported by Llama-2/3 and Mistral, and the memory win
on the KV cache is substantial. Workloads dominated by short
prompts and small batches see a smaller GQA win and may prefer
full MHA.

\begin{provedassumed}[Attention block: what is established vs assumed]
\textbf{Proved.} Permutation-equivariance of raw attention (no
positional signal); the score decomposition
$A V = \mathrm{softmax}(QK^{\top}/\sqrt{d_k} + M) V$ is exact;
causal masking by $-\infty$ strictly zeros post-softmax weights at
$j > i$; KV-cache formula Eq.~\eqref{eq:week4-kvbytes} is an exact
byte count. \\
\textbf{Empirical.} Head specialization (attention circuits,
induction heads) \citep{elhage2021mathematical, olsson2022induction}.
GQA quality near-parity with MHA \citep{ainslie2023gqa} ---
benchmark-contingent. Attention-sink behaviour: first-token
residual mass accumulation is observed, not derived. \\
\textbf{Assumed.} A4.1's independence/unit-variance assumption for
the $\sqrt{d_k}$ derivation holds at initialization; the scaling
persists empirically despite the assumption breaking mid-training.
A4.3: head specialization is training-outcome dependent and should
not be treated as an architectural contract.
\end{provedassumed}

\subsection{Feed-Forward Blocks}

Between attention layers each position is transformed independently
by a two-layer feed-forward network (FFN):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week4-ffn-classic}
\mathrm{FFN}(x) \;=\; W_2 \,\sigma(W_1 x + b_1) + b_2,
\end{equation}
with $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{d_f \times d}$, $W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d_f}$,
and typically $d_f = 4d$. The activation $\sigma$ started as ReLU and
has been superseded by smoother variants (GeLU, Swish/SiLU) for
training stability.

\subsubsection{GLU Variants and SwiGLU}

\citet{shazeer2020glu} showed that \emph{gated} linear units improve
quality with no extra parameters if the hidden size is rescaled
appropriately:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week4-swiglu}
\mathrm{SwiGLU}(x)
 \;=\; \bigl(W_g x \odot \mathrm{Swish}(W_u x)\bigr) W_2,
\qquad
\mathrm{Swish}(z) = z\,\sigma(z),
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid and $\odot$ is elementwise. SwiGLU is
the default in Llama, PaLM, and most modern decoder-only models. It
uses three projection matrices ($W_g, W_u, W_2$) per layer rather
than two; to keep parameter count fixed, $d_f$ is typically reduced
from $4d$ to $\tfrac{8}{3}d$.

The FFN performs most of the parameter count of a Transformer layer
(roughly $2/3$ of the layer parameters in a classical block). In
current interpretability work it is viewed as a key-value memory:
the first matrix's rows act as ``keys'' matched against the
residual-stream activation, and the second matrix's columns act as
``values'' written back.

\subsection{The Transformer Block, Pre-Norm vs Post-Norm}

\subsubsection{Pre-Norm is the Modern Default}

The original Transformer used post-norm (normalize after the residual
add):
\begin{equation}
x_{\ell+1} \;=\; \mathrm{LN}\bigl(x_\ell + \mathrm{Sublayer}(x_\ell)\bigr).
\end{equation}
The \emph{pre-norm} variant, now standard, moves the normalization
inside the residual branch:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week4-prenorm}
x_{\ell+1} \;=\; x_\ell + \mathrm{Sublayer}\bigl(\mathrm{LN}(x_\ell)\bigr).
\end{equation}
\citet{xiong2020onlayer} showed that, under standard initialization
and the warmup-style training recipes of that era, pre-norm yields
stable training without the warmup phase that post-norm requires,
because the residual path remains an identity map at initialization.
The stability advantage is recipe-conditional: post-norm with
sufficient warmup and careful initialization can also train
successfully (and was the original Transformer's choice). Pre-norm is the
configuration used by GPT-2 onwards, Llama, PaLM, and essentially all
modern LLMs. Figure \ref{fig:week4-block} shows a pre-norm block.

\subsubsection{LayerNorm and RMSNorm}

Layer normalization \citep{ba2016layernorm} normalizes each token's
activation along the feature dimension:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{LN}(x) \;=\; \gamma \odot \frac{x - \mu(x)}{\sigma(x) + \epsilon} + \beta,
\end{equation}
with $\mu, \sigma$ the per-token mean and std. RMSNorm
\citep{zhang2019rmsnorm} drops the mean-centering:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week4-rmsnorm}
\mathrm{RMSNorm}(x) \;=\; \gamma \odot \frac{x}{\mathrm{RMS}(x) + \epsilon},
\qquad
\mathrm{RMS}(x) = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{d}\sum_j x_j^2},
\end{equation}
saving $\sim\!7\%$ of the normalization compute with no quality loss
in practice. Llama family models use RMSNorm.

\subsubsection{The Residual Stream}

A useful mental model, crystallized in
\citet{elhage2021mathematical}: the residual stream is a
$d$-dimensional vector per token, carried unchanged across layers
\emph{except} that each attention or FFN sublayer \emph{reads} from it
(via the pre-norm) and \emph{writes} back into it (via the residual
add). This additive structure is what makes Transformers
interpretable-ish: circuits are built by decomposing writes into the
residual stream.

\subsection{Positional Encoding}

Attention is permutation-equivariant: a permutation of rows of $X$
permutes the rows of $\mathrm{Attn}(Q, K, V)$ identically. Without
positional information the model cannot tell ``dog bites man'' from
``man bites dog''. There are four common approaches.

\subsubsection{Sinusoidal (Absolute)}

The original Transformer \citep{vaswani2017attention} adds a fixed
sinusoid to the token embedding:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week4-sinusoidal}
\mathrm{PE}(t, 2i)     = \sin\!\Bigl(t \,/\, 10000^{2i/d}\Bigr),
\quad
\mathrm{PE}(t, 2i+1)   = \cos\!\Bigl(t \,/\, 10000^{2i/d}\Bigr).
\end{equation}
Different feature dimensions correspond to different wavelengths,
spanning roughly $2\pi$ to $10000 \cdot 2\pi$. The motivation:
$\sin(A+B)$ and $\cos(A+B)$ can be written as linear combinations of
$\{\sin A, \cos A, \sin B, \cos B\}$, so the model can represent
relative offsets by linear operations on the encoding. Sinusoidal
encodings support extrapolation (a form never seen at training time
is still a valid sinusoid), but only weakly.

\subsubsection{Learned Absolute}

BERT \citep{devlin2019bert} and early GPT models use a learned
$T_{\max} \times d$ table of positional vectors, looked up like an
embedding. Simple and effective up to the training $T_{\max}$, but
cannot extrapolate beyond it.

\subsubsection{ALiBi}

Attention with Linear Biases \citep{press2022alibi} adds a
\emph{fixed} linear penalty to the attention scores based on relative
distance:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week4-alibi}
\mathrm{Score}(i, j) \;=\; \frac{q_i^{\top} k_j}{\sqrt{d_k}}
  \;-\; m_h\,|i - j|,
\end{equation}
with a head-specific slope $m_h > 0$. ALiBi adds \emph{no} extra
parameters and extrapolates naturally to sequences longer than
training.

\subsubsection{Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE)}

RoPE \citep{su2021rope}, the default in Llama, Mistral, and many
contemporary LLMs, encodes position by \emph{rotating} the query and
key vectors in 2-D subspaces by angles that depend on position.
Partition the head dimension into $d_k / 2$ pairs. For position $t$
and pair index $i$, define
\begin{equation}
\theta_i \;=\; 10000^{-2i/d_k},
\qquad
R_{t, i} \;=\; \begin{pmatrix}
\cos(t\theta_i) & -\sin(t\theta_i) \\
\sin(t\theta_i) &  \cos(t\theta_i)
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The RoPE-rotated query is $\tilde q_t = R_t q_t$; similarly for $k_t$.

\begin{derivation}[RoPE relative-position identity]
A 2-D rotation matrix $R_\theta$ satisfies
$R_\theta^{\top} = R_{-\theta}$ (transpose flips the sign of the
off-diagonal sines) and $R_\alpha R_\beta = R_{\alpha+\beta}$
(rotations compose by angle addition). Applied to a block-diagonal
$R_t = \mathrm{diag}(R_{t\theta_1}, \dots, R_{t\theta_{d_k/2}})$ ---
where each 2-D block rotates independently by its own frequency
$\theta_i$ --- both identities hold blockwise, hence
$R_t^{\top} R_s = R_{-t} R_s = R_{s-t}$. Therefore
\begin{align}
(\tilde q_t)^{\top} \tilde k_s
&= (R_t q_t)^{\top} (R_s k_s) \\
&= q_t^{\top} R_t^{\top} R_s k_s \\
&= q_t^{\top} R_{-t} R_s k_s
  \qquad (\text{transpose} = \text{inverse rotation}) \\
&= q_t^{\top} R_{s-t} k_s.
\label{eq:week4-rope-relative}
\end{align}
The score depends only on the \emph{relative} displacement
$s - t$, not on absolute positions. \Ident\ Exact under exact
arithmetic. (A4.2): extrapolation beyond training range is
\emph{not} a corollary --- it relies on empirical generalization of
learned $q, k$ statistics to unseen rotation angles.
\end{derivation}

This is the key formal property RoPE guarantees: relative-position
information is baked into every pre-softmax score, without adding
parameters. RoPE interacts cleanly with FlashAttention (the rotation
is applied to $q$ and $k$ before the score, so the kernel does not
see it) and has become the de facto standard.

\subsubsection{Positional Encoding Decision Matrix}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|l|}
\hline
Scheme & Relative? & Extrapolates? & Parameters? \\
\hline
Sinusoidal abs. & weakly & weakly  & no \\
Learned abs.    & no     & no      & $T_{\max}\,d$ \\
Relative (Shaw) & yes    & limited & small \\
ALiBi           & yes    & yes (within tested ranges) & $H$ slopes \\
RoPE            & yes    & only with NTK/YaRN scaling & no \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{provedassumed}[Positional encoding: what is established vs assumed]
\textbf{Proved.} Attention is permutation-equivariant without a
positional signal (algebraic). RoPE's relative-position identity
Eq.~\eqref{eq:week4-rope-relative} is exact under exact arithmetic
(2-D rotation algebra). ALiBi's distance bias
Eq.~\eqref{eq:week4-alibi} is a zero-parameter additive penalty. \\
\textbf{Empirical.} ALiBi extrapolation beyond training length is
benchmark-supported at the lengths measured in
\citet{press2022alibi}; RoPE extrapolation requires NTK-aware or YaRN
interpolation to remain usable. Sinusoidal encodings ``support''
extrapolation weakly in the sense that the signal is still a valid
sinusoid, but effective context quality drops sharply beyond train
length. \\
\textbf{Assumed.} A4.2: learned $Q,K$ statistics generalize across
unseen rotation angles. This fails measurably at long-context
extrapolation without explicit surgery on $\{\theta_i\}$. Production
long-context RoPE deployments must evaluate at target context
length, not rely on the identity alone.
\end{provedassumed}

\subsection{The KV Cache}

Autoregressive generation produces one token at a time. At step $t+1$
the model needs to compute $Q_{t+1} K_{1:t+1}^{\top}$ and
$A_{t+1} V_{1:t+1}$. Re-computing $K$ and $V$ for all preceding
tokens at every step is $O(T^2)$ redundant work. The KV cache
\emph{stores} the key/value tensors from previous steps and appends
the current step's $(K_t, V_t)$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week4-kvcache}
K_{1:t} \;\leftarrow\; \begin{bmatrix} K_{1:t-1} \\ k_t \end{bmatrix},
\qquad
V_{1:t} \;\leftarrow\; \begin{bmatrix} V_{1:t-1} \\ v_t \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Per-token inference then costs $O(T d_k)$ for attention (one row of
$Q$ against a $T$-row $K$), down from $O(T^2 d_k)$.

\subsubsection{KV Cache Memory Math}

The cache stores $K$ and $V$ for every position and layer. Under
MHA there are $H$ independent K/V tensors per layer; under GQA with
$G$ groups, there are only $G$. The factor of 2 counts both $K$ and
$V$. The general formula is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week4-kvbytes}
\text{bytes/token}
\;=\; 2 \cdot L \cdot G_{\mathrm{eff}} \cdot d_k \cdot b,
\qquad
G_{\mathrm{eff}} = \begin{cases}
H & \text{MHA}, \\
G & \text{GQA}, \\
1 & \text{MQA}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
\Ident\ Substituting $G_{\mathrm{eff}}$ recovers the MHA, GQA, and MQA
cases as a single equation. The dense-vs-GQA compression ratio is
$H/G$: Llama-2-70B uses $H=64$, $G=8$, cutting KV cache by $8\times$.

\begin{workedexample}[KV cache budgets: dense MHA vs GQA vs MQA]
Fix $L=80$, $H=64$, $d_k=128$, $b=2$~(bf16), and compute
bytes/token from Eq.~\eqref{eq:week4-kvbytes}:
\begin{align*}
\text{MHA} \; (G_{\mathrm{eff}}=64) &:
  \;2 \cdot 80 \cdot 64 \cdot 128 \cdot 2 \approx 2.62~\text{MB/tok},\\
\text{GQA}, G=8 \; (G_{\mathrm{eff}}=8) &:
  \;2 \cdot 80 \cdot 8 \cdot 128 \cdot 2 \approx 0.33~\text{MB/tok},\\
\text{MQA} \; (G_{\mathrm{eff}}=1) &:
  \;2 \cdot 80 \cdot 1 \cdot 128 \cdot 2 \approx 41~\text{KB/tok}.
\end{align*}
At 128k context, \emph{per request}: MHA $\approx 336$~GB (larger
than the weights at $\approx 140$~GB --- unusable); GQA $\approx
42$~GB (tractable on an H100); MQA $\approx 5.3$~GB (multi-tenant
serving friendly at some quality cost).
\end{workedexample}

This is why GQA, KV quantization, and KV eviction policies
(e.g., StreamingLLM, attention-sink attention) have become
first-order engineering concerns.

\subsection{FlashAttention}

A naive attention implementation materializes the full
$T \times T$ score matrix $A$ in HBM (GPU global memory) and reads it
back to compute $AV$. For large $T$, memory bandwidth -- not FLOPs --
is the bottleneck. FlashAttention \citep{dao2022flashattention,
dao2023flashattention2} restructures the computation to avoid
materializing $A$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Tile $Q, K, V$ into blocks that fit in on-chip SRAM.
  \item Compute each output row's attention in place using an
        \emph{online softmax} that maintains a running max and sum,
        updating them as new key-tiles arrive.
  \item Write only the final $O \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_v}$ back
        to HBM.
\end{itemize}
The result is I/O-optimal up to a constant on the GPU memory
hierarchy assumed by the original analysis (HBM-bound attention
on contemporary NVIDIA accelerators); it is also \emph{exact} (not
an approximation). FlashAttention provides $2\text{--}4\times$
speedups on long contexts and, more importantly, reduces the
\emph{attention-intermediate} activation memory (the $T \times T$
score and probability tensors) from $O(T^2)$ to $O(T)$. Other
activations in the block (residual stream, FFN intermediates) are
unaffected; the savings unlock longer-context training but do not
remove all $T$-dependent activation memory. It is an object lesson in systems
engineering: no change to the mathematical model, a large change in
performance, achieved by matching computation to the memory
hierarchy.

\subsection{Attention Patterns and Interpretability Pitfalls}

Visualizing the attention matrix $A$ is a common interpretability
practice: plot attention weights on a heatmap, highlight what each
token ``looks at.'' \Emp\ \citet{jain2019attention} constructed
adversarial attention distributions that produce the same output
from different attention patterns --- a demonstration, not a
theorem, but one that undermines any causal reading of the heatmap.
\Heur\ In production, treat heatmaps as telemetry (degeneracy
detection, attention-sink monitoring), not as mechanistic
explanations (A4.3).

Three more principled alternatives, developed largely in the
Transformer circuits line of work
\citep{elhage2021mathematical, olsson2022induction}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Weight-space analysis}: examine
        $W_Q^{\top} W_K$ and $W_O W_V$ matrices and their interaction
        through the residual stream.
  \item \textbf{Induction-head discovery}: an algorithmic signature
        of in-context learning is a two-layer composition that copies
        the token following a previous occurrence of the query.
  \item \textbf{Attribution patching}: replace activations at each
        layer with a counterfactual and measure the change in the
        output logit, yielding a causal (not just correlational)
        attribution.
\end{itemize}
For production purposes: \emph{do not} claim attention heatmaps
explain model behaviour to users or regulators. Use them as
engineering telemetry (are heads degenerate? do heads attend to
\texttt{<bos>} excessively -- a known ``attention sink'' phenomenon?),
not as causal claims.

\subsection{Human Factors and Failure Modes}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{``Attention is explanation''}. As discussed, it is
        not. Users and reviewers should not treat attention heatmaps
        as mechanistic accounts.
  \item \textbf{Context-length marketing}. Quoted ``context windows''
        often lose effective quality long before the advertised
        maximum because of KV-cache approximations, anisotropy in
        positional encoding, and loss-landscape artefacts at train
        time. Measure on your workload; don't trust datasheet claims.
  \item \textbf{Attention sinks}. Models trained with pre-norm tend
        to dump residual mass onto the first few tokens (especially
        \texttt{<bos>}); removing them at inference produces drastic
        quality loss. This is a known, not-fully-understood phenomenon.
  \item \textbf{Positional brittleness}. RoPE extrapolation beyond the
        training length (``long context'') requires interpolation
        tricks (NTK-aware, YaRN) -- quality is not automatic.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Week 4 Takeaway}

\begin{quote}
The Transformer turns sequence modeling into parallel, differentiable
memory access. Its mathematical simplicity -- a handful of matrix
multiplies glued together by a softmax and a residual stream -- is
what makes it scalable, but its engineering surface area is vast:
positional encoding, normalization placement, head-sharing, KV-cache
management, and I/O-aware kernels each determine whether a model can
be trained at scale and served at cost.
