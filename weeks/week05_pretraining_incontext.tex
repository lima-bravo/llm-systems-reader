\section{Pretraining, GPT-Style Models, and In-Context Learning}
\label{sec:week5-pretrain}

\subsection*{Setting the Scene}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Setting the Scene}

\paragraph{The problem.}
Picture an engineer in 2018, holding a fresh Transformer
implementation and a hard drive of crawled web text. The model
runs. The text is there. The question staring back at the
engineer is what to do with the text in order to obtain a model
that is, in some useful sense, fluent. The classical answer was to pick a
narrow task --- translation, summarization, sentiment classification
--- and supervise the model on labelled examples of that task. The
trouble with this answer was that labelled examples are expensive,
that any single task is a thin slice of what language can be used
for, and that a model trained only on translation has no obvious
reason to be good at anything else. We wanted, instead, a single
training procedure that produced a single model that was, in some
broad sense, capable. The procedure that emerged is called
\emph{pretraining}: take an enormous unlabelled corpus, ask the
model to predict the next token in that corpus, and trust that, in
order to predict the next token well, the model will be forced to
acquire a great deal of useful structure --- syntax, world
knowledge, arithmetic, reasoning, the layout of a Python script.
The trust, it turned out, was not entirely misplaced.

\paragraph{How we got here.}
Predicting the next token is an old idea. Claude Shannon, in 1951,
estimated the entropy of English by playing a game in which human
subjects guessed the next letter of a withheld passage. The
performance of the guessers gave him a lower bound on the
predictability of the language. He treated the guessers as a model.
The connection between language modelling and information theory
has been with us ever since. What was missing for half a century
was a sufficiently expressive learner and a sufficiently large
corpus. Both arrived together in the 2010s. By the time GPT-2
appeared in 2019, the recipe had stabilized: take a decoder-only
Transformer, train it on a heroically filtered slice of the open
web, and minimize cross-entropy on the next-token prediction. By
the time GPT-3 appeared in 2020 \citep{brown2020language}, the
field had a phenomenon to explain. Tom Brown and his collaborators
showed that a sufficiently large pretrained model could perform
new tasks --- arithmetic, translation, question answering --- with
no parameter updates at all, simply by being shown a few examples
in the prompt. They called this \emph{in-context learning}, and
nobody, including the authors, fully understood why it worked.

The other thread to follow is the question of how big to make the
model and how much to train it. Jared Kaplan and his collaborators
at OpenAI, in 2020, plotted loss against parameter count, dataset
size, and compute, and found smooth power laws over many orders of
magnitude. The empirical regularity was reassuring; the prescription
that followed was that, given a fixed compute budget, you should
spend most of it on parameters. Two years later, Jordan Hoffmann
and the Chinchilla team at DeepMind reanalyzed the question with
better experiments and showed that Kaplan's recommended ratio was
substantially off: for compute-optimal training you should scale
parameters and tokens roughly together, not parameters faster than
tokens. The Chinchilla paper changed how the field budgeted
compute, and most modern open models reflect its prescription.

\paragraph{Where we stand.}
A modern pretraining run takes a curated corpus on the order of
trillions of tokens, packs it into long sequences with attention
masks at document boundaries, and trains a decoder-only Transformer
under cross-entropy with a cosine-decayed learning rate. The
objective itself --- predict the next token --- has not changed
since GPT-1. What has changed is the scale, the data quality, and
the engineering. At inference time the same model is sampled rather
than argmax-decoded, with one of a small zoo of strategies (greedy,
beam, temperature, top-$k$, nucleus / top-$p$, min-$p$) that trade
off determinism against diversity. Few-shot prompting, despite the
absence of any clear theoretical foundation, remains a workhorse
technique: you put a handful of examples in the context and let the
model continue the pattern.

\paragraph{What goes wrong.}
Cross-entropy training on internet-scale text has costs that the
loss function itself does not see. The training data contains
copyrighted material, personal information, and a great deal of
text that the model will, under the right pressure, regurgitate
verbatim --- a phenomenon called \emph{memorization}, with its
own measurement literature \citep{carlini2021extracting,
carlini2023quantifying}. The training data also contains the
prejudices of its authors, which the model will reproduce
fluently. The model will be confidently wrong about things its
training data was confidently wrong about, and will be plausibly
wrong about things its training data did not contain. Few-shot
prompting is brittle: changing the order of the examples, the
formatting of the labels, or even the choice of separator can shift
accuracy by tens of points, and these effects have no principled
fix. The much-celebrated phenomenon of \emph{emergent capabilities}
--- abilities that appear suddenly at scale --- has been argued by
\citet{schaeffer2023mirage} to be partly an artefact of metric
choice rather than a fact about model behavior. None of this means
the recipe does not work. It means the recipe works in a way that
is harder to characterize than its champions sometimes admit, and
the math in this chapter exists to help you tell the difference.

\subsection{Conceptual Core: Pretraining as Representation Learning}

GPT-style pretraining optimizes a single objective: maximize the
autoregressive log-likelihood of text under the causal factorization
from Chapter~1:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week5-pretrain-obj}
\mathcal{L}(\theta)
 \;=\; -\,\E_{x \sim \mathcal{D}}
       \Bigl[\,\textstyle\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})\Bigr].
\end{equation}
No task labels appear; all ``task'' structure is latent in the
distribution $\mathcal{D}$. The conceptual consequence:
\begin{quote}
Training produces a general-purpose sequence model;
prompting selects a behavior from it.
\end{quote}
This is the single most important systems insight of the era. A
pretrained LLM is not a chatbot, a classifier, or a code assistant;
it is a statistical engine over token sequences that can be steered
toward any of those behaviors by appropriate context. Every
downstream deployment (Weeks 7--11) is, at bottom, a mechanism for
constructing the right prompts and constraining the outputs of this
engine.

\subsection{Notation and Standing Assumptions}
\label{sec:week5-notation}

\begin{notationbox}
For this chapter: $N$ is parameter count, $D$ is training tokens, and
$C$ is training compute in FLOPs. Sequence length is $T$ (reused for
context length and, where noted, sampling temperature; we disambiguate
by context). Position index is $t \in \{1, \ldots, T\}$. The
vocabulary has size $V$ and $\mathbf{z} \in \R^{V}$ denotes pre-softmax
logits at a given position; $p(\cdot)$ is the resulting token
distribution. Sampling parameters: temperature $T_s > 0$ (written $T$
in decoding formulas by convention), top-$k$ cutoff
$k \in \{1, \ldots, V\}$, nucleus threshold $p \in (0, 1]$, min-$p$
coefficient $\alpha \in (0, 1)$, repetition-penalty $\rho \ge 1$.
Beam width is $B$. For ICL few-shot prompts, $k$-shot denotes the
number of in-context exemplars (distinguished from top-$k$ by
context). For memorization, $\ell$ is the recall string length and
$k$ is duplication count (reusing symbol intentionally — see
Eq.~\eqref{eq:week5-memorization}).

Standing assumptions are:
\textbf{(A5.1)} Chinchilla exponents $(\alpha, \beta)$ and the
compute-optimal form $(N^\star, D^\star) \propto C^{1/2}$ are imported
from Chapter~2 (assumption A2.2) and extrapolate only within the fit
range.
\textbf{(A5.2)} Mechanistic hypotheses for in-context learning
(implicit Bayesian inference, induction heads, gradient descent in
the forward pass) are \emph{interpretive hypotheses} with partial
empirical support on controlled tasks; none is a proved theorem for
natural-language ICL.
\textbf{(A5.3)} The memorization law Eq.~\eqref{eq:week5-memorization}
is an \emph{empirical parametric fit} to extraction-probe data; its
exponents $(a, b, c)$ vary by model family, corpus, and probe
methodology, and the functional form is not derived from first
principles.
\end{notationbox}

\subsection{Autoregressive, Masked, and Prefix Language Modeling}

Three objectives have competed for the LLM crown; the decoder-only
autoregressive objective has won for capability reasons that are
worth understanding.

\subsubsection{Autoregressive (Causal) LM}

Eq.~\eqref{eq:week5-pretrain-obj}, with a causal mask (Chapter~5).
Every position is both a training \emph{context} and a training
\emph{target}: the cross-entropy is summed over all $T$ positions in a
single forward pass. Architectures: GPT family, Llama, Mistral,
Claude, Gemini, PaLM (with minor variants).

\subsubsection{Masked LM (MLM)}

Used in BERT~\citep{devlin2019bert}. Replace a fraction $p$ (typically
15\%) of input tokens with a \texttt{[MASK]} sentinel and predict the
originals. Bidirectional context (no causal mask) makes MLM excellent
for representation learning -- BERT embeddings powered the 2018-2020
NLP ecosystem -- but the model cannot \emph{generate} without
auxiliary machinery.

\subsubsection{Prefix LM / Span Corruption}

T5~\citep{raffel2020t5} and UL2 train an encoder-decoder with
\emph{span corruption}: contiguous spans of the input are replaced
with sentinel tokens, and the decoder reconstructs them. This
unifies MLM and autoregressive LM in a single objective. Gemini and
some multilingual models retain a prefix-LM component.

\subsubsection{Why Autoregressive Won}

Three practical reasons:
(i)~it generalizes to arbitrary prompt $\to$ completion tasks at
inference time, so one pretrained model can serve all workloads;
(ii)~it trains densely (loss per position, not per masked token),
squeezing more signal from each forward pass;
(iii)~it scales cleanly under the Chinchilla law without
encoder/decoder partitioning. The engineering convenience -- one
forward pass, one objective, one KV cache -- dominates.
Figure~\ref{fig:pretraining_objectives} shows the three objectives
operating on the same sentence.

\subsection{The Pretraining Data Pipeline}

\subsubsection{Acquisition}

The dominant starting point is \emph{Common Crawl}, a monthly
snapshot of the public Web currently totalling $\sim\!400$\,TB of
WARC files. Commercial pretraining mixes in curated sources: code
repositories (GitHub, StackOverflow), books (Project Gutenberg,
licensed collections), scientific papers (arXiv, PubMed),
encyclopedia (Wikipedia), Q\&A forums (Reddit, StackExchange), and
dialogue corpora. Open-source reference pipelines: the
Pile~\citep{gao2020pile} at 825\,GB; RedPajama~\citep{redpajama2023} as
a 1.2\,T-token Llama-style mix.

\subsubsection{Filtering}

Raw Common Crawl is approximately $3\%$ usable text by volume. A
typical filter cascade:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Language identification} (fastText / CLD3) -- drop
        non-target languages.
  \item \textbf{Quality classifiers} -- a small model trained to
        distinguish ``book-like'' from ``spam-like'' text.
  \item \textbf{Heuristic filters} -- minimum mean line length,
        punctuation ratio, top-word ratio (Gopher filters).
  \item \textbf{Toxicity / safety filters} -- keyword and
        classifier-based removal of CSAM, extremist content, PII.
  \item \textbf{Perplexity filtering} -- use a small reference model
        to drop extreme-perplexity outliers (both garbage and
        memorized).
\end{enumerate}
Each filter has a false-positive rate that disproportionately removes
low-resource languages, minority dialects, and specialized domains.
Filter choices are a primary driver of the cultural biases an LLM
inherits.

\subsubsection{Deduplication}

\citet{lee2022deduplicating} showed that near-duplicate training
examples cause disproportionate memorization and, counterintuitively,
\emph{worse} generalization. Modern pipelines apply:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Exact dedup}: hash-match 13-gram shingles across the
        corpus.
  \item \textbf{Near-duplicate dedup}: MinHash-LSH at the document
        level, typically at Jaccard threshold $\ge 0.8$.
  \item \textbf{Eval contamination checks}: explicit 13-gram matching
        against benchmark test sets (MMLU, GSM8K, HumanEval) with
        removal.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Licensing and Provenance}

A major operational issue in 2024--2026: much Common-Crawl content is
copyrighted; some jurisdictions permit text-and-data mining, others
require opt-in. Responsible pipelines now maintain a per-document
provenance log (source URL, crawl date, license detection, opt-out
signals), and the regulatory surface (EU AI Act, US state laws,
individual suits) is rapidly evolving. This becomes a Chapter~11
governance input: your assurance case requires a defensible data
provenance record.

\subsubsection{Red-Flag Detection}

Production pipelines also scan for: CSAM signatures, known PII
corpuses, adversarial data-poisoning signatures, and data engineered
to bypass filters. This is a moving adversarial front.

\subsection{Batch Packing and Sequence-Length Schedules}

\subsubsection{Packing}

Documents have variable lengths but Transformers operate on
fixed-length batches. Padding wastes FLOPs. The canonical solution is
\emph{packing}: concatenate multiple short documents into a single
sequence of length $T$, separated by the end-of-document token
(\texttt{<|endoftext|>}, a.k.a.\ \texttt{<eos>}). Combined with a
\emph{block-diagonal} attention mask (Chapter~5), attention cannot leak
across documents within the same packed sequence, preserving the
per-document autoregressive factorization.

\subsubsection{Sequence-Length Schedules}

Training at the full context length throughout pretraining is
wasteful: short-context phases are cheap and establish the base
capabilities; long-context capability can be added at the end by
continued pretraining at extended $T$. A common schedule:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Phase 1: $T = 2048$ for $\sim\!90\%$ of tokens.
  \item Phase 2: $T = 8192$ for $\sim\!8\%$ of tokens.
  \item Phase 3: $T = 32\,768$ (or longer) for the final $\sim\!2\%$
        of tokens, often with an adjusted RoPE base frequency
        (``NTK-aware scaling'').
\end{itemize}
This staged strategy underlies the long-context variants of the
Llama and Code Llama families and several other open-weight models
released through 2024, achieving extended context at a fraction of
the compute a uniform full-length pretrain would cost.

\subsection{Scaling Laws Revisited: Compute-Optimal Training}

From Chapter~2, the Chinchilla joint law~\citep{hoffmann2022chinchilla}
is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week5-chinchilla}
L(N, D) \;=\; E + \frac{A}{N^\alpha} + \frac{B}{D^\beta}.
\end{equation}

\begin{derivation}[compute-optimal $(N^\star, D^\star)$, recap of Chapter~2]
Fix $C \approx 6\,N D$. Minimizing Eq.~\eqref{eq:week5-chinchilla}
subject to $N D = C/6$ via the Lagrangian
$\mathcal{J}(N, D, \mu) = L(N, D) - \mu\,(N D - C/6)$ yields the
first-order conditions
\[
\alpha\,A\,N^{-\alpha-1} = \mu\,D,
\qquad
\beta\,B\,D^{-\beta-1} = \mu\,N.
\]
Eliminating $\mu$ gives
$\alpha A\,N^{-\alpha} = \beta B\,D^{-\beta}$, fixing the ratio of the
two loss terms at the optimum. Combined with the budget constraint
this produces
\[
N^\star \propto C^{a},
\quad
D^\star \propto C^{b},
\quad
a = \frac{\beta}{\alpha + \beta},
\quad
b = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}.
\]
For Chinchilla's fitted $\alpha \approx 0.34$, $\beta \approx 0.28$,
$a \approx b \approx 0.5$, recovering the
``roughly 20 tokens per parameter'' rule.
\end{derivation}

Under the compute budget $C = 6 N D$, the minimizing allocation is
therefore $N^\star \propto C^{1/2}, D^\star \propto C^{1/2}$, i.e.,
\emph{roughly 20 training tokens per parameter} at current fitted
constants. For the Chinchilla recipe's compute budget of
$C \approx 5.9 \times 10^{23}$ FLOPs (i.e.\ $6 \times 70\text{B}
\times 1.4\text{T}$), this yields $N \sim 70$\,B and $D \sim 1.4$\,T
tokens.

\Heur\ \emph{Why this matters operationally}: the cost of an LLM is
dominated by \emph{training} compute for frontier labs and by
\emph{inference} compute for deployed services. A
\emph{Chinchilla-undertrained} large model is wasteful for training
but \emph{cheap to serve}; a \emph{Chinchilla-overtrained} small
model is cheap to train per quality point but expensive per
inference. Production choices (Llama-3 at 15T tokens for the 8B
model) explicitly overtrain to optimize serving economics. This is a
cost-engineering decision, not a scientific one.

\subsection{In-Context Learning}

\subsubsection{The Empirical Result}

\citet{brown2020language} showed that a 175\,B-parameter
autoregressive LM, given a prompt of the form
\begin{quote}\small\ttfamily
Q: \{example question 1\}\\
A: \{answer 1\}\\
\ldots\\
Q: \{example question k\}\\
A: \{answer k\}\\
Q: \{query question\}\\
A:
\end{quote}
can perform many tasks with accuracy rivaling fine-tuned small models.
No gradient update occurs; the examples act as \emph{context}. This
is \emph{in-context learning (ICL)}. ICL exhibits three striking
empirical properties:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Zero/few-shot performance improves steeply with model scale.
  \item It is brittle to prompt format and example order
        \citep{lu2022fantastically}.
  \item Much of the ``learning'' is contributed by the \emph{format}
        (the label space, the template), not by the specific input-output
        pairings \citep{min2022rethinking}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Mechanistic Hypotheses}

Three research lines explain ICL at different levels of abstraction.

\textbf{Implicit Bayesian inference}
\citep{xie2022explanation}. Pretraining fits a mixture model over
latent ``tasks''; at inference time the prompt's examples are
evidence conditioning on which the model computes the posterior over
task identity. The model then generates from the posterior-weighted
mixture. Strength: explains format sensitivity (ambiguous examples
give diffuse posteriors). Weakness: the ``latent task'' construct is
not directly observable.

\textbf{Induction heads} \citep{olsson2022induction}. A two-layer
Transformer circuit, discovered empirically in every sufficiently
trained autoregressive LM, implements the rule ``given
\texttt{...A B...A}, copy \texttt{B}'' through the composition of a
\emph{previous-token head} (layer 1) and a \emph{copy-on-match head}
(layer 2). The emergence of this circuit during training coincides
with a sharp drop in in-context loss. Strength: mechanistically
grounded in the architecture of Chapter~5. Weakness: extends only to
the copy-like component of ICL.

\textbf{Gradient descent in the forward pass}
\citep{garg2022transformers,vonoswald2023transformers}. A single
Transformer layer can be constructed to implement one step of
gradient descent on a linear-regression problem given the
regression examples as input; multi-layer stacks can implement
multi-step updates. On simple function classes, trained Transformers
converge to the same solutions as the corresponding GD-in-the-
forward-pass circuits. Strength: gives a crisp computational target.
Weakness: extrapolation to natural-language ICL is non-trivial.

No single hypothesis suffices on its own; the three are best read as
complementary views of a phenomenon that is empirically rich and
mechanistically heterogeneous. For a survey, see
\citet{dong2023survey}.

\begin{provedassumed}
\emph{Proved (within scope).}
(i) On linear-regression toy settings, a Transformer \emph{can} be
constructed whose forward pass implements one step of GD on the
in-context examples~\citep{vonoswald2023transformers}; this is a
\emph{constructive possibility theorem}, not a claim about what
pretrained LLMs actually do.
(ii) Induction-head circuits can be \emph{identified} via
attribution patching in trained autoregressive LMs, and their
emergence coincides with a loss drop attributable to in-context
copying~\citep{olsson2022induction}; this is a \emph{mechanistic
observation}, not a capability theorem.

\emph{Assumed.}
(i) That implicit-Bayesian-inference, induction-head, and
GD-in-the-forward-pass hypotheses \emph{scale} to natural-language
ICL in frontier models. Evidence exists in each case but is
strongest for controlled tasks; extrapolation to open-ended language
tasks is interpretive.
(ii) That ICL is ``learning'' in any operational sense. No parameter
update occurs; the phenomenon is \emph{conditional distribution
selection} under A5.2.
(iii) That ICL quality on a benchmark is model-stable: in practice
prompt order, separators, and label verbalization can move accuracy
by 20~points~\citep{lu2022fantastically}, so the ``ICL accuracy'' of
a given model is itself a distribution over prompts, not a scalar.
\end{provedassumed}

\subsection{Few-Shot Format Sensitivity}

Lu et al.~\citep{lu2022fantastically} showed that simply
\emph{reordering} the in-context examples can move accuracy by 20
percentage points on common tasks. Three engineering-relevant
findings:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Accuracy varies substantially with example order, separator
        choice, and label verbalization.
  \item Adding irrelevant examples sometimes \emph{improves} accuracy
        by reinforcing format; sometimes it hurts.
  \item The optimal prompt is model-specific and version-specific.
\end{itemize}
Operational countermeasures:
(i)~include prompts in the version-pinned model contract;
(ii)~run prompt regression tests as part of CI;
(iii)~for critical paths, run self-consistency or majority-voting
across several paraphrases of the same prompt.

\subsection{Sampling and Decoding}

Recall from \S\ref{sec:week5-notation} the decoding parameter set
$(T_s, k, p, \alpha, \rho, B)$: temperature, top-$k$ cutoff, nucleus
threshold, min-$p$ coefficient, repetition penalty, beam width. All
operate on the per-position logit vector $\mathbf{z} \in \R^{V}$.

Given logits $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^V$ at each step, the
conditional distribution after temperature $T$ scaling is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week5-softmax-temp}
p_T(i) \;=\; \frac{\exp(z_i / T)}{\sum_j \exp(z_j / T)}.
\end{equation}
$T \to 0$ recovers greedy (argmax), $T = 1$ recovers the model's
native distribution, $T > 1$ flattens it. (Throughout this section,
$T$ is temperature; sequence length from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:week5-pretrain-obj} does not appear in the decoding
formulas.)

\subsubsection{Greedy and Beam}

\textbf{Greedy} takes the argmax each step. Deterministic, cheap, and
frequently degenerate on open-ended generation (loops, repetition)
\citep{holtzman2020curious}. \textbf{Beam search} maintains the
top-$B$ partial hypotheses by cumulative log-probability and returns
the highest-scoring completion. Beam is effective for
\emph{constrained} generation (translation, code completion with a
finite correct answer) but produces bland, generic text on open-ended
tasks because the joint log-probability it maximizes is a poor
proxy for human-preferred output.

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Top-$k$ and Top-$p$ (Nucleus)}{Top-k and Top-p (Nucleus)}}

\textbf{Top-$k$} \citep{fan2018hierarchical}: restrict the softmax to
the $k$ highest-probability tokens, renormalize, sample.

\textbf{Top-$p$ (nucleus)} \citep{holtzman2020curious}: let
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week5-nucleus}
\mathcal{V}^{(p)}
 \;=\; \mathrm{smallest~set}\ S \subseteq V
       \ \mathrm{s.t.}\ \sum_{i \in S} p_T(i) \ge p,
\end{equation}
restrict the distribution to $\mathcal{V}^{(p)}$, renormalize,
sample. Nucleus adapts the candidate set size to the distribution's
concentration: sharp distributions (confident contexts) have small
$|\mathcal{V}^{(p)}|$; flat distributions (ambiguous contexts) have
large $|\mathcal{V}^{(p)}|$.

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{Min-$p$}{Min-p}}

\citet{nguyen2024minp} introduced \emph{min-$p$} sampling: keep any
token whose probability is at least $\alpha \cdot p_{\max}$, where
$p_{\max}$ is the top-1 probability and $\alpha \in (0, 1)$. This
handles heavy-tail distributions more robustly than top-$p$ at high
temperatures and has seen rapid adoption in 2024--2025 local-model
inference stacks.

\begin{workedexample}[top-$k$ vs top-$p$ vs min-$p$ on identical logits]
Fix the post-temperature distribution over five tokens
$\{w_1, \ldots, w_5\}$ at $T = 1$:
\[
p \;=\; (0.50,\; 0.25,\; 0.15,\; 0.06,\; 0.04).
\]
The three cutoffs behave differently because each discretizes a
different feature of the distribution.

\textbf{Top-$k$ with $k = 3$.} Keep the three highest-probability
tokens $\{w_1, w_2, w_3\}$ with pre-normalization masses
$(0.50, 0.25, 0.15)$, total $0.90$. Renormalized to
$(0.556, 0.278, 0.167)$; $w_4$ and $w_5$ get probability zero.

\textbf{Top-$p$ (nucleus) with $p = 0.9$.}
Sort by descending probability; the smallest prefix whose cumulative
mass reaches $0.9$ is $\{w_1, w_2, w_3\}$ (cumulative
$0.50 + 0.25 + 0.15 = 0.90$). Same kept set as top-$k$ for this
distribution, but this is \emph{coincidence}: shift mass to
$(0.50, 0.10, 0.10, 0.15, 0.15)$ and top-$p = 0.9$ would keep
$\{w_1, w_4, w_5, w_2\}$ (reordering by probability to cumulative
$0.90$) while top-$k = 3$ would keep $\{w_1, w_4, w_5\}$.

\textbf{Min-$p$ with $\alpha = 0.1$.} The threshold is
$\alpha \cdot p_{\max} = 0.1 \cdot 0.50 = 0.05$. Kept tokens are
those with $p_i \ge 0.05$: $\{w_1, w_2, w_3, w_4\}$, masses
$(0.50, 0.25, 0.15, 0.06)$, total $0.96$. Renormalized to
$(0.521, 0.260, 0.156, 0.063)$. Min-$p$ retains $w_4$ that top-$k=3$
and top-$p=0.9$ both dropped, because $w_4$ is still within $10\times$
of the top.

\textbf{Under temperature reshape.} Raise temperature to $T_s = 2$
and re-softmax the underlying logits (not shown); the distribution
flattens, $p_{\max}$ falls, so min-$p$'s threshold falls with it and
its kept set \emph{grows} automatically — this is the behavior that
distinguishes min-$p$ from top-$p$ under high-$T$ sampling. Top-$p$
at fixed $p = 0.9$ would also grow but more slowly; top-$k$ at fixed
$k$ stays constant regardless of entropy, which is why pure top-$k$
is a poor default.
\end{workedexample}

\subsubsection{Repetition Penalty}

A post-hoc logit modification:
\begin{equation}
\tilde z_i \;=\; \begin{cases}
z_i / \rho & \text{if $i$ has appeared in the context}, \\
z_i        & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
with $\rho > 1$ discouraging exact repetition. Simple, effective on
loopy generations, but can degrade fluent text. Cleaner alternatives
include \emph{frequency} and \emph{presence penalties} (OpenAI API)
and \emph{DRY} (don't repeat yourself) penalty scoped to recent
context.

\subsection{Emergence: Real or Metric Artefact?}

\Emp\ \citet{wei2022emergent} reported that several LLM capabilities
exhibit sharp, unpredictable jumps as model scale increases, which
they termed \emph{emergent abilities}. This is an \emph{observed
scaling curve on specific metrics}, not a claim about underlying
capability.

\Ident\ \citet{schaeffer2023mirage} replied that much of the
apparent ``emergence'' is a \emph{metric effect}: a discontinuous
scoring rule (e.g., exact string match) applied to a smoothly
improving per-token distribution will itself exhibit a sharp
threshold, because any strictly positive per-token error probability
$\epsilon$ yields a sequence-level error probability
$1 - (1-\epsilon)^{\ell}$ that transitions rapidly from near-1 to
near-0 as $\epsilon$ declines past $\sim 1/\ell$ for sequence length
$\ell$. Switching to a continuous, calibrated metric (log-likelihood,
token-level accuracy) usually reveals smooth scaling trajectories
consistent with the scaling laws of \S\,Scaling Laws.

\begin{workedexample}[metric continuity inverts the emergence picture]
Suppose a per-token error probability $\epsilon$ declines smoothly
with compute as $\epsilon(C) = \epsilon_0 (C_0/C)^{\gamma}$ for some
$\gamma > 0$ — a standard Chinchilla-style power law. Consider a
task whose grading rule is exact match over $\ell = 10$ tokens.

\textbf{Per-token accuracy (continuous).}
$\mathrm{acc}_{\mathrm{tok}}(C) = 1 - \epsilon(C)$ is a smooth
monotone function of $\log C$; plotted on log-compute axes it is
nearly linear in the regime of interest.

\textbf{Exact-match accuracy (discontinuous).}
$\mathrm{acc}_{\mathrm{EM}}(C) = (1 - \epsilon(C))^{\ell}$. For
numerical concreteness pick $\ell = 10$ and sweep
$\epsilon \in \{0.5, 0.3, 0.2, 0.1, 0.05, 0.01\}$:
\[
\mathrm{acc}_{\mathrm{EM}} \;\in\;
\{9.8\!\times\!10^{-4},\; 0.028,\; 0.107,\; 0.349,\; 0.599,\; 0.904\}.
\]
The per-token accuracy changed by a factor $\sim 2$ across the sweep
(from $0.50$ to $0.99$); the exact-match accuracy changed by a factor
$\sim 10^{3}$ and crossed the ``useful'' threshold of $0.5$ somewhere
between $\epsilon = 0.1$ and $\epsilon = 0.05$. Plotted as a function
of compute this looks like a sudden emergence; in reality the
underlying per-token trajectory is smooth. This is exactly
\citet{schaeffer2023mirage}'s argument made concrete.
\end{workedexample}

\Cons\ When a capability appears to ``emerge'' during scale-up,
first check whether the metric is continuous in the underlying
distribution. If not, switch metrics before making causal claims.
For safety-relevant claims (e.g., ``the model cannot do $X$ at our
scale''), conservative practice assumes smooth scaling and budgets
for the predicted performance of a larger model under the same
metric. Governance (Chapter~11) should require that any
``non-emergence'' argument used in an assurance case be replicated
under at least one continuous metric; a single-metric
non-capability claim is not evidence.

\subsection{Memorization and Extraction}

\citet{carlini2021extracting} demonstrated that a 1.5\,B-parameter
GPT-2 could be induced to emit verbatim strings from its training
corpus, including PII. \citet{carlini2023quantifying} quantified the
memorization rate as a function of:
(i)~model scale (larger models memorize more),
(ii)~data duplication (repeated strings memorize more), and
(iii)~prompt length (longer prompts extract more).

\Emp\ Empirically — under A5.3 — and \emph{within the
low-extraction regime probed by} \citet{carlini2023quantifying},
memorization of a string of length $\ell$ scales roughly as the
parametric fit
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:week5-memorization}
\Pr[\text{verbatim recall}]
  \;\approx\; \min\!\left(1,\;
              \left(\frac{N}{N_0}\right)^{a}
              \left(\frac{k}{k_0}\right)^{b}
              \left(\frac{\ell}{\ell_0}\right)^{c}\right),
\end{equation}
with parameter count $N$, duplication count $k$, and length $\ell$,
and fitted exponents $a, b, c > 0$. The product form is a
local-fit description of the low-probability regime and saturates
trivially at $1$; for high-probability regimes a logit-link or odds
form is the safer extrapolation.

\Assum\ Eq.~\eqref{eq:week5-memorization} is a regression to
extraction-probe data; the exponents $(a, b, c)$ depend on model
family, training mixture, and attacker methodology, and the
multiplicative functional form is not derived from first principles
but fitted because it captures the dominant scaling pattern across
probes~\citep{carlini2023quantifying}. Do not extrapolate outside the
probe regime without new measurement.

\Cons\ This has three operational consequences:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Aggressive deduplication is \emph{the} first-line memorization
        defence.
  \item Red-team extraction attacks must be part of V\&V for
        PII-sensitive workloads.
  \item Output filters alone are insufficient: the training data
        itself must be treated as a privacy asset.
\end{itemize}
Chapter~11 (Governance) returns to memorization as a residual risk in
the assurance case.

\subsection{Human Factors and Failure Modes}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Anthropomorphizing ICL}. Users perceive the model as
        ``learning'' their preferences from examples; the model
        forgets across turns unless the examples are re-included.
  \item \textbf{Overtrusting few-shot performance}. Few-shot results
        are order-sensitive and model-version-sensitive; silent
        regressions occur on API upgrades.
  \item \textbf{Fluency as correctness}. Fluent-but-wrong outputs
        read more plausibly than hesitant-but-right outputs;
        calibration probes (Chapter~1) are essential.
  \item \textbf{Contamination-as-capability}. A benchmark the model
        has already seen can create the illusion of generalization.
        Cutoff-date discipline matters.
  \item \textbf{Extraction surprise}. Users who paste sensitive
        strings into prompts of a third-party API should treat the
        strings as disclosed; the training loop is a ratchet.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Practitioner Lab}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Mini-pipeline}. Download a 100\,MB Common Crawl WARC
        slice, run language-ID, perplexity filtering, and MinHash-LSH
        dedup. Report retention rates at each stage.
  \item \textbf{Eval contamination check}. For a small open model,
        compute the rate at which 13-grams from MMLU test items
        appear in its training data (proxy: Pile). Relate to
        reported benchmark deltas.
  \item \textbf{Batch packing}. Implement document packing with a
        block-diagonal mask; verify numerically that a packed
        sequence yields the same loss as the same documents unpacked.
  \item \textbf{Few-shot order sensitivity}. Choose a classification
        benchmark. Run 24 random permutations of the same 4-shot
        prompt; plot accuracy distribution.
  \item \textbf{Induction-head discovery}. On a 12-layer open model
        (Pythia-70M suffices), identify a head that matches the
        induction-head signature and confirm it via attribution
        patching.
  \item \textbf{Sampling sweep}. For a single open-ended prompt,
        generate 100 samples each at $T \in \{0.3, 0.7, 1.0, 1.3\}$
        and $p \in \{0.7, 0.9, 0.95\}$. Compute distinct-$n$ and
        self-BLEU; plot the diversity-quality Pareto frontier.
  \item \textbf{Emergence stress test}. Pick a ``emergent'' task
        reported in \citet{wei2022emergent}. Replace exact-match with
        token-level log-likelihood; re-examine the scaling curve.
  \item \textbf{Memorization probe}. For a small open model with a
        known training corpus, probe for verbatim recall of 50-token
        strings. Plot recall rate vs duplication count.
\end{enumerate}

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Chapter 6 Takeaway}

\begin{quote}
Pretraining creates a powerful statistical engine; prompting steers
it, but does not retrain it. ``In-context learning'' is a misleading
name for a phenomenon that is better described as distribution-
conditional generation: the model is inferring which latent task you
mean from the context, and it makes that inference with the
biases, brittleness, and memorization residues that its pretraining
data instilled.
\end{quote}
